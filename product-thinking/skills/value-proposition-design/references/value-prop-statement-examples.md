# Value Proposition Statements — Good vs Bad

The VP statement compresses the canvas into a single, testable claim. This reference shows good, weak, and bad statements across categories.

## Template recap

```
For [specific customer segment]
who [context / trigger / job],
our [product category]
provides [top 1-2 gain creators]
and relieves [top 1-2 pains]
unlike [named alternative],
because [the reason your value map is different].
```

## Example 1 — B2B SaaS: async standup tool

### Bad

> "The best way to do standups."

Issues: slogan, not a claim. No segment. No alternative. No differentiator. Cannot be tested against reality.

### Weak

> "Our async standup tool helps remote teams stay aligned."

Issues: "remote teams" is too broad. No specific job. "Stay aligned" is vague. No alternative. No differentiator.

### Good

> For **engineering leads of distributed teams across three or more time zones** who **run weekly standups that require every member to attend live**, our **async standup tool** provides **a 20-minute decision digest** and relieves **the fatigue of early-morning or late-evening attendance**, unlike **Zoom meetings with rotating schedules**, because **we structure written updates to be skim-read, not replayed.**

What works: one segment (not "remote teams" — specifically distributed across 3+ zones, engineering leads). Context (weekly standups requiring live attendance). One gain (20-min decision digest). One pain relieved (off-hours fatigue). Named alternative (Zoom with rotating schedules). Differentiator ("structure updates to be skim-read").

Testable: a team member can restate it; a team member can evaluate whether it applies to them.

## Example 2 — Consumer app: habit tracker

### Bad

> "Build better habits."

Issues: tagline, not a VP. Every competitor could claim this.

### Weak

> "Our app helps you build habits by letting you track them daily."

Issues: "tracking" is a feature, not a differentiator. No segment. No alternative named.

### Good

> For **people in their first 90 days of forming a new habit** who **abandon most tracking apps after two weeks**, our **habit tracker** provides **one-tap logging under three seconds** and relieves **the guilt spiral that starts with missing a single day**, unlike **streak-based apps like [X]**, because **we count weekly ratio, not consecutive days, so one miss doesn't restart the streak.**

What works: the segment is specific (first 90 days, app-abandoners). Context (the vulnerable window). Specific gain (one-tap under 3 seconds). Specific pain (the guilt spiral from missed day). Named alternative. The differentiator is concrete and mechanical (weekly ratio vs. streak).

The differentiator is the strongest element: it's a specific mechanism, not a claim about outcomes.

## Example 3 — Developer tool: one-click sandbox

### Bad

> "The best way to onboard new hires."

### Weak

> "Our sandbox tool gives new developers a safe environment."

### Good

> For **engineering leads onboarding new hires** who **need to give them a working dev environment by end of day one without giving them production credentials**, our **one-click sandbox** provides **a realistic, production-like environment** and relieves **the 2-day manual setup that blocks new hires from being productive**, unlike **sharing personal dev environments or waiting for IT provisioning**, because **we auto-provision from an anonymized snapshot of production schema, with no prod-credential exposure.**

What works: the segment is narrow (leads onboarding new hires, not "developers"). The context is specific (day one, prod-credential restriction). Named alternatives cover the two real choices (shared personal env, IT provisioning). The differentiator is mechanical and defensible (anonymized snapshot + no prod creds).

## Example 4 — Marketplace: freelance designers

### Bad

> "Connecting designers with clients."

### Weak

> "Find designers for your small business."

### Good

> For **owners of small businesses with marketing budgets under $10k/year** who **need one-off design work (logos, packaging, website graphics)**, our **curated designer marketplace** provides **matched designers vetted by portfolio review** and relieves **the hours of browsing and filtering on generic marketplaces**, unlike **Fiverr or Upwork**, because **we pre-screen designers for specific verticals (SaaS, DTC, restaurant) and match based on stated vertical, not keyword search.**

What works: segment scoped by budget and business type. Specific job (one-off design, not ongoing). Alternative named (Fiverr, Upwork). Differentiator is concrete (vertical pre-screening, not keyword matching).

## Anti-patterns — specific failure modes

### Anti-pattern 1: "unlike other tools"

"Unlike other tools, we do X better." Useless because "other tools" is un-testable. Always name the alternative.

Fix: name one specific competitor or the default workaround.

### Anti-pattern 2: "because we care"

"Because we obsess over the customer experience." Not a mechanism. Every competitor says it. Un-falsifiable.

Fix: the "because" must state a mechanism the customer can understand or test — a design choice, a data asset, a partnership, a technical approach.

### Anti-pattern 3: segment is "everyone who does X"

"For everyone who struggles with email." Too broad to act on.

Fix: narrow by role, context, or size. "For CEOs of companies with 10-50 employees who spend more than 3 hours a day in email."

### Anti-pattern 4: gains that are really features

"Provides a dashboard." A dashboard is a feature, not a gain. The gain is what the dashboard produces — "visibility into team blockers within 10 seconds of opening."

Fix: state gains as outcomes the customer experiences.

### Anti-pattern 5: pains that are really wants

"Relieves the pain of not having a mobile app." A missing mobile app is a feature gap, not a pain. The pain is the underlying friction — "the pain of not being able to respond to customer questions when away from your desk."

Fix: state pains as user-experienced friction, not absent features.

## Writing process

1. Start with one segment. If two segments need different statements, write two.
2. Fill the template slot by slot. Force yourself to name the alternative.
3. Compress to one sentence (or two short ones). Longer than that signals vagueness.
4. Read aloud. If it sounds like a slogan, rewrite.
5. Show to someone in the segment. Can they restate it? Do they agree it applies?
6. If no, refine. If yes, use it in landing pages, sales decks, and internal alignment.

## Testing the statement

A good VP statement passes three tests:

1. **Restatement test**: a member of the segment can restate it in their own words.
2. **Alternative test**: the segment can explain why your alternative is worse than yours (not just different).
3. **Falsification test**: if your product stopped delivering the gain or relieving the pain, would the segment notice? If yes, the statement is grounded. If no, the claim is weak.
