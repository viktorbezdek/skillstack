# Editing Examples — All Four Passes

Real-shape first drafts, fully edited. Each example shows the four passes applied in order: compress, active voice, strip hedges, fix nominalizations. The goal is to make the transformation visible so the technique transfers.

## Example 1 — Quarterly update email

### First draft (163 words)

"Hi everyone, I hope this message finds you well. I wanted to take a moment to reach out and share an update with the team regarding our Q2 performance and some thoughts on what might lie ahead for us in Q3. As you may or may not be aware, we have been making a concerted effort to improve our activation metrics, and I think it would be fair to say that we have seen some encouraging movement in that direction. There are a number of factors that we believe could potentially be contributing to this trend, but the truth of the matter is that more investigation is needed before we can be sure. With regard to Q3, it seems like there may be an opportunity for us to possibly double down on the initiatives that have been working, although we should probably also give some consideration to whether a course correction might be appropriate for the initiatives that have not been performing as well as we had hoped."

### Pass 1 — compress (108 words, -34%)

"Hi everyone. I wanted to share a Q2 update and Q3 thoughts. We've been working to improve activation, and we've seen encouraging movement. Several factors may be contributing, but we need more investigation to know. For Q3, there's an opportunity to double down on what's working, though we should also consider course-correcting the initiatives that haven't performed as well as we hoped."

### Pass 2 — active voice (107 words)

"Hi everyone. I'm sharing a Q2 update and Q3 thoughts. We've been working to improve activation, and we're seeing encouraging movement. Several factors may contribute, but we need more investigation to know. For Q3, we should double down on what's working, and course-correct the initiatives that haven't performed as well as we hoped."

### Pass 3 — strip hedges (82 words, -50% from draft)

"Hi everyone. Q2 update and Q3 thoughts: activation is improving — several factors may contribute and we're investigating. For Q3, double down on what's working and course-correct the rest."

### Pass 4 — fix nominalizations (unchanged here)

Already verb-forward. No nominalizations remain.

### Final (82 words, 50% reduction)

"Hi everyone. Q2 update and Q3 thoughts: activation is improving — several factors may contribute and we're investigating. For Q3, double down on what's working and course-correct the rest."

**What changed structurally:** subject/purpose came first (BLUF). The narrative "I wanted to take a moment to reach out" disappeared. The hedging cloud ("may or may not be aware", "could potentially be", "it would be fair to say") evaporated. What remains is claims and actions.

## Example 2 — Technical status Slack message

### First draft (94 words)

"Hey team, just wanted to give you all a quick update on where we are with the migration. So we've been working on this for a couple of weeks now and I think we're making some good progress overall. There were some initial concerns about compatibility with the old data format that we have been trying to address, and I would say we're maybe about 70% of the way through that particular challenge. We should probably be able to finish up the remaining work within the next sprint or two, assuming nothing unexpected comes up."

### Pass 1 — compress (65 words)

"Migration update: we've been working for two weeks. Initial compatibility concerns with the old data format — we're ~70% through addressing them. Finishing work should complete within 1-2 sprints, barring surprises."

### Pass 2 — active voice (63 words)

"Migration update: we're two weeks in. Initial compatibility concerns with the old data format — we've addressed ~70%. Remaining work completes in 1-2 sprints, barring surprises."

### Pass 3 — strip hedges (52 words, -45%)

"Migration update: two weeks in. Compatibility concerns with old data format — 70% resolved. Remaining work completes in 1-2 sprints, barring surprises."

### Pass 4 — fix nominalizations (unchanged)

No nominalizations.

### Final (52 words)

"Migration update: two weeks in. Compatibility concerns with old data format — 70% resolved. Remaining work completes in 1-2 sprints, barring surprises."

**What changed:** "I think", "I would say", "maybe", "should probably be able to" — all cut. Concrete number (70%) retained. Hedge "barring surprises" retained because it marks real uncertainty.

## Example 3 — Paragraph from an engineering RFC

### First draft (128 words, heavy nominalization)

"The implementation of the new caching layer will require the performance of a migration of all existing session data from our current storage solution to the newly-designated cache infrastructure. This migration will necessitate the creation of a data synchronization mechanism that will allow for the maintenance of consistency between the two systems during the transition period. Upon completion of the migration and verification that all data has been successfully transferred, the deprecation of the legacy storage can then be performed. The estimated duration for the entire process is anticipated to be approximately two weeks, although it should be noted that this estimate could potentially be affected by the emergence of unforeseen issues during the execution phase."

### Pass 1 — compress (91 words, -29%)

"Implementing the new cache requires migrating existing session data from current storage. The migration needs a data sync mechanism to maintain consistency during transition. After migration and verification, we can deprecate the legacy storage. Estimated duration: two weeks, though this could be affected by unforeseen issues during execution."

### Pass 2 — active voice (85 words)

"The new cache requires migrating session data from current storage. We need a data sync mechanism to maintain consistency during transition. After migration and verification, we deprecate the legacy storage. Estimated duration: two weeks, though unforeseen issues could extend it."

### Pass 3 — strip hedges (72 words)

"The new cache requires migrating session data from current storage. We need a data sync mechanism to maintain consistency during transition. After migration and verification, we deprecate the legacy storage. Estimated duration: two weeks; unforeseen issues could extend it."

### Pass 4 — fix nominalizations (61 words, -52% from draft)

"The new cache requires migrating session data from current storage. A data sync mechanism keeps the two consistent during transition. After migrating and verifying, we deprecate the legacy storage. Estimated duration: two weeks; unforeseen issues could extend it."

### Final (61 words)

"The new cache requires migrating session data from current storage. A data sync mechanism keeps the two consistent during transition. After migrating and verifying, we deprecate the legacy storage. Estimated duration: two weeks; unforeseen issues could extend it."

**What changed:** "implementation of" → "implementing", "performance of a migration" → "migrating", "creation of a mechanism" → "a mechanism", "maintenance of consistency" → "keeps consistent", "upon completion of" → "after", "deprecation can then be performed" → "we deprecate". Nominalizations collapsed, and the passage became concrete.

## Example 4 — Customer-facing announcement

### First draft (152 words)

"We are writing to let you know that, effective May 1st of this year, there will be some changes being made to the pricing structure of our product that we believe will better reflect the value that our users are getting from the platform. Specifically, we will be transitioning from our current per-seat pricing model to a usage-based model that takes into consideration the number of API calls that are being made by each customer on a monthly basis. We understand that this may be cause for some concern among certain segments of our user base, and we want to assure you that we have given careful consideration to this decision and that we believe it is in the best interests of all of our customers in the long run. Please feel free to reach out to our customer success team if you have any questions or concerns."

### Pass 1 — compress (92 words, -39%)

"Effective May 1st, we're changing our pricing model. We're moving from per-seat to usage-based pricing — billed monthly on API call volume. We understand this may concern some customers. We considered this decision carefully and believe it serves customers better long-term. Contact our customer success team with questions."

### Pass 2 — active voice (85 words)

"Effective May 1st, we're changing our pricing model. We're moving from per-seat to usage-based — billed monthly on API call volume. We understand this may concern some customers. We considered this carefully and believe it serves customers better long-term. Contact our customer success team with questions."

### Pass 3 — strip hedges (76 words — keeping warmth)

"Effective May 1st, we're changing our pricing model from per-seat to usage-based, billed monthly on API call volume. We understand this is a significant change. We considered it carefully and believe it serves customers better long-term. Contact our customer success team with questions."

### Pass 4 — fix nominalizations (unchanged)

No significant nominalizations remain.

### Final (76 words, 50% reduction from draft)

"Effective May 1st, we're changing our pricing model from per-seat to usage-based, billed monthly on API call volume. We understand this is a significant change. We considered it carefully and believe it serves customers better long-term. Contact our customer success team with questions."

**What changed:** Preamble ("We are writing to let you know that...") removed. Hedges ("may be cause for concern among certain segments") replaced with direct acknowledgment ("this is a significant change"). Stylistic hedging ("we want to assure you that") replaced with simpler direct address.

**What was kept:** The acknowledgment that this is significant (warmth, customer-facing voice). The offer to contact customer success. Hedges serving politeness in a sensitive context were retained deliberately.

## Pattern summary

Across the four examples, the typical transformation:

| Pass | Typical savings |
|---|---|
| Compression | 25-40% |
| Active voice | 1-5% |
| Hedge removal | 10-20% |
| Nominalization fixes | 5-15% |
| **Total** | **40-60%** |

First drafts usually shrink by about half without losing meaning. If your transformation produces more than 60% reduction consistently, your first drafts have a pattern of padding worth investigating.

## What stays, always

- Concrete numbers
- Named people and entities
- Specific dates and deadlines
- Warmth where warmth is required (customer-facing, sensitive news)
- Hedges that mark real uncertainty with a source

## What goes, almost always

- "In order to"
- "Due to the fact that"
- "Moving forward"
- "At this point in time"
- "I would like to take this opportunity"
- "It is important to note that"
- Stacked modal verbs ("might potentially be able to")
- Corporate jargon ("synergies", "leverage")
- Pleasantries as preamble
- "In my opinion" (when opinion is implied)
