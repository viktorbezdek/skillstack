# Narrative Fundamentals

> A sequence of events becomes a story only when those events are causally linked. This file covers the essential tests and elements that separate narrative from chronology, with concrete examples for writers, designers, and communicators working in any medium.

---

## The causality test

The fastest diagnostic for whether you have a story or a list is the conjunction test, popularized by Matt Stone and Trey Parker (South Park) as the foundation of their own plotting method.

**Chronology** connects events with *and then*:
> The king died. **And then** the queen died.

**Narrative** connects events with *because of this* or *but therefore*:
> The king died. **Because of this**, the queen died of grief.

The causal link is not decorative — it is what creates meaning. Chronology reports; narrative explains. A pitch deck that lists quarterly milestones is chronology. A pitch deck that shows how each decision created pressure that forced the next one is narrative.

**Practical test:** Replace every transition in your sequence with "and then." If this feels accurate and complete, you do not yet have a story.

## The four core elements

Every story, regardless of length or medium, contains four elements. When any one is absent, the story collapses into a different form.

| Element | What it is | What happens without it |
|---|---|---|
| **Character with a want** | A specific entity pursuing a specific goal | You have a setting, not a story |
| **Obstacle** | Something that prevents straightforward achievement of the want | You have a plan, not a story |
| **Stakes** | What is lost if the want is not achieved | You have a scene, not a story |
| **Transformation** | The character changes (or refuses to change) because of what happens | You have an incident, not a story |

The transformation element is frequently omitted in business and technical storytelling, which is why case studies and retrospectives so often feel flat. Something happened; no one became different because of it.

**Transformation can also be negative or refused.** A character who faces every demand for growth and refuses it is itself a story — the tragedy form. The absence of transformation must be chosen and earned; it cannot be an accident.

## Stakes — internal, external, philosophical

Dan Harmon's framing of story (built on Campbell and Vogler) identifies three layers of stakes that the best stories run simultaneously.

**External stakes:** The concrete, visible goal. Will the detective catch the killer? Will the company survive the acquisition? External stakes drive plot and give audiences something to follow.

**Internal stakes:** The psychological or emotional cost. If the detective solves the case by becoming the thing she hunts, does winning mean anything? Internal stakes drive character arc and create complexity.

**Philosophical stakes:** The larger truth the story is making an argument about. Is the world one where justice is possible, or one where corruption always wins? Philosophical stakes give the story meaning beyond its characters.

A story with only external stakes is a thriller. A story with external and internal stakes is a drama. A story with all three layers is literature.

In practice, even short-form storytelling (product narratives, case studies, talks) benefits from at least two layers. Most practitioners work only the external layer and wonder why their audience does not care.

## Specificity is power

The single most common failure in narrative writing is abstraction where specificity is required.

**Abstract:**
> A user struggled to complete the checkout process and gave up.

**Specific:**
> Maria, checking out on her phone while waiting for her kids' soccer practice to end, hit the address form on step three. The autofill dropped the wrong city. She typed the correction, hit Next, and the form cleared entirely. She closed the tab.

The specific version contains a character with a life, an obstacle with texture, and stakes that are visible without being stated. The abstract version is a data point. Neither is lying — but only one creates a story the audience will carry.

Specificity applies at every scale: the concrete detail in a sentence, the named character in a scene, the named company in a case study. The instinct to generalize (to protect privacy, to appear objective, to seem broadly applicable) is often the instinct that kills the story.

## The "so what?" test

Every story must survive the question: *so what?* This is not cynicism. It is the test of whether the story has communicated stakes to the audience.

If a reader or listener can ask "so what?" and feel satisfied by the silence, the story has not made its stakes legible. The stakes may exist in the writer's head. They have not reached the audience.

The test is applied at the end, but the failure is usually at the beginning. Stories that fail the "so what?" test almost always lack one of two things: a character the audience has been given a reason to care about, or stakes that have been made concrete before the climax arrives.

## What narrative is NOT

These forms are frequently confused with story, especially in professional contexts.

| Form | What it is | What it lacks |
|---|---|---|
| **Chronology** | Events in sequence | Causality |
| **Description** | Properties of a person, place, or thing | Change over time |
| **Character sketch** | Traits and background of a person | Want, obstacle, transformation |
| **Backstory** | History explaining how a situation came to be | Present-tense conflict and stakes |
| **Worldbuilding** | Rules, setting, lore of a constructed world | Characters with wants and obstacles |
| **Argument** | Logical case for a conclusion | Dramatic tension and a character who changes |

Knowing what category your material falls into tells you what it needs. A product brief that is currently a description needs a character and a want. A talk that is currently an argument needs a protagonist whose journey enacts the argument's conclusion.
