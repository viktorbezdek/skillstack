> A speech is not a document read aloud. It is a time-bound performance where the audience cannot go back and reread, so structure and repetition carry a weight they do not carry in text. This file covers frameworks for designing and delivering spoken presentations — from short pitches to keynote-length talks.

---

## The Medium Changes Everything

Text allows rereading. A reader who misses a point can return. A listener cannot. This asymmetry forces specific compensations in spoken delivery:

| Text | Speech |
|------|--------|
| Reader controls pace | Speaker controls pace |
| Reader can re-read | One pass only |
| Dense structure can work | Must be signposted heavily |
| Footnotes carry detail | Repetition carries detail |
| Five sub-points are fine | Three is the maximum |

The compensations are: deliberate repetition, heavy signposting ("I am about to argue X; here is why"), concrete examples in place of abstract claims, the rule of three, and planned pauses before important lines.

## Nancy Duarte's Sparkline

From *Resonate* (2010). Every great speech alternates between two states:

- **What is** — current reality, the status quo, what the audience already accepts
- **What could be** — the ideal future, the transformation being proposed

The contrast produces tension. Tension produces engagement. Engagement produces action.

Visualized as a line oscillating above and below a baseline — above for "what could be," below for "what is." The pattern is not a straight line; it rises and falls repeatedly, each oscillation deepening the audience's investment in the gap.

**Famous examples:**

- *MLK's "I Have a Dream" (1963)* — "What is": segregation, brutality, poverty. "What could be": children of former slaves and former slave-owners sitting together, justice rolling down like waters. The alternation accelerates toward the end, peaks with the dream sequence, and lands at the call to "let freedom ring."
- *Steve Jobs, iPhone launch (2007)* — "What is": every phone sucks in specific ways. "What could be": one device, no stylus, it just works. Jobs returned to the gap repeatedly before the reveal.

**Practical application:** Write your speech as two columns. Left: everything you say about current reality. Right: everything you say about the ideal future. If one column dominates, you have a lecture or a fantasy, not a speech.

## TED 18-Minute Structure

The 18-minute limit is not arbitrary. Research cited by TED curator Chris Anderson suggests 18 minutes is long enough to develop a complete argument and short enough to hold full attention. Many TED talks follow a recognizable structure:

| Segment | Duration | Purpose |
|---------|----------|---------|
| Hook | 30 sec | Surprise, question, or striking fact |
| Credibility | 30 sec | Why this speaker, why now |
| Core idea | 2 min | The single thing to remember |
| Evidence and stories | 10 min | 2–4 supporting narratives with data |
| Practical application | 3 min | What the audience can do |
| Call to action / close | 2 min | Emotional landing and specific ask |

**On the hook:** The worst opening is "Today I want to talk about X." The best openings drop the audience into a specific moment, a counterintuitive claim, or a question they silently answer. The hook does not need to state the thesis — it needs to create forward pull.

**On the core idea:** TED's internal term is "idea worth spreading." One sentence, no conjunctions. If you cannot say it without "and" or "but," you have two ideas. Cut one.

**On evidence and stories:** Data without a story is forgettable. A story without data is unverifiable. The 10-minute center of a TED talk typically alternates: story → data → story → data. The stories make data memorable; the data gives stories credibility.

## Monroe's Motivated Sequence

Alan Monroe, Purdue University, 1935. Still the gold standard for persuasive speeches. Five steps:

1. **Attention** — open with something that earns focus. This is the hook.
2. **Need** — establish the problem with specificity. Use data, personal story, or both. Make the audience feel the problem, not just understand it.
3. **Satisfaction** — present the solution. Be concrete. Not "we need more investment in education" but "a $200 increase in per-pupil spending in Title I schools, applied to early literacy programs, produces a 0.4 grade-level improvement by third grade."
4. **Visualization** — help the audience inhabit the world after the solution. Show the positive future if they act; show the deteriorating present if they do not. Duarte's sparkline is the visual equivalent of this step.
5. **Action** — tell them exactly what to do, right now. "Sign the petition at the link below." "Talk to your manager this week." "Vote on Tuesday." Vague calls to action ("let's make a difference") produce no behavior change.

Monroe's sequence works because it mirrors the structure of motivated human decision-making: attention precedes desire; desire without a path to satisfaction is frustration; satisfaction without a compelling image of the future produces no urgency; urgency without a specific action dissipates.

## Aristotelian Rhetoric

Three proofs, from *Rhetoric* (~350 BC):

| Proof | Definition | Modern translation |
|-------|-----------|-------------------|
| **Ethos** | Credibility of the speaker | Why should they trust you |
| **Pathos** | Emotional engagement | What they should feel |
| **Logos** | Logical argument | What the facts support |

**The common misunderstanding:** that logos is most important because audiences are rational. Contemporary behavioral research (Kahneman, Haidt, Cialdini) confirms what Aristotle argued: pathos drives action, ethos determines whether the audience suspends skepticism, and logos primarily defeats counter-arguments after the audience has already been moved.

**Practical balance:**

- Lead with ethos (establish credibility in the first 90 seconds)
- Build with pathos (the stories that carry the middle)
- Close with logos (the data that makes the commitment feel safe)

A speech with strong logos but weak ethos will be dismissed as clever but untrustworthy. A speech with strong pathos but weak logos will be inspiring but forgettable by Tuesday. All three must be present.

## Opening Patterns That Work

| Pattern | Example |
|---------|---------|
| Concrete specific story | "Three years ago I was standing in an ICU at 2 a.m. watching a machine breathe for my father." |
| Surprising statistic with personal frame | "Ninety percent of startups fail. I have personally been in four of them." |
| Question the audience silently answers | "How many of you have ever said something you immediately wished you could take back?" |
| Disarming admission | "I don't have the answer to this problem. But I know where the answer is hiding." |
| Artifact | Hold up an object. Let the silence work. Then explain it. |

**Openings that reliably fail:**

- "I'm so honored / excited / happy to be here" (throat-clear)
- Reading your own bio (the host does this; you should not)
- Stating your agenda: "Today I'll cover three things: first… second… third…"
- Apologizing: "I only have 20 minutes so I'll go fast"

The first 30 seconds determine whether the audience decides to listen or to wait politely. Do not spend them on housekeeping.

---

## Closing Patterns That Work

| Pattern | Purpose |
|---------|---------|
| Callback to the opening | Creates satisfying structural symmetry ("close the loop") |
| Specific ask | Names exactly what the audience should do |
| Image or object that encapsulates the argument | Creates a lasting mental anchor |
| Quote | Only if the quote is better than your own prose — and it usually is not |

**Closings that reliably fail:**

- "Thank you, any questions?" (hands the energy to the room with no landing)
- Drifting off ("…so, yeah, that's kind of the idea, I guess")
- Re-summarizing ("So to recap, today we covered…") — this is for classrooms, not speeches

The close is the last thing the audience carries out of the room. Write it before you write the middle.

## Pacing and Pauses

A silence of 3–5 seconds feels eternal to the speaker. To the audience it registers as gravitas, confidence, and emphasis. Planned pauses serve two functions:

1. **Before** a key line: creates anticipation, signals importance
2. **After** a key line: allows absorption, prevents the key line from being buried by what comes next

Speakers who never pause signal anxiety. The audience inherits the anxiety. Pacing fast communicates that you do not trust the material or the audience.

**Practical rule:** Write the word PAUSE into your notes at every important moment. Practice stopping. The discomfort passes; the effect does not.

## Memorization vs. Memorizing the Skeleton

Do not memorize word-for-word.

A memorized speech sounds memorized. When something breaks the pattern — an audience question, a dropped thought, a technical failure — the speaker loses the thread entirely because they have stored words, not structure.

**What to memorize instead:**

1. The skeleton: the five to seven beats of the speech in order
2. The exact wording of your five to six most important lines (opening line, core idea line, CTA line, and two to three emotional peaks)
3. The transitions between sections

Practice aloud, not in your head. The mouth and the brain store language differently. A speech practiced silently will feel alien when spoken.

## Rule of Three

Humans remember groups of three. This is not a guideline — it is a cognitive constraint.

- "Life, liberty, and the pursuit of happiness"
- "Government of the people, by the people, for the people"
- "Faster, better, cheaper"

When you have more than three points, group them into three meta-categories. When you have two points, find a third or collapse them into one. When you find yourself saying "there are seven things you need to know," your audience will remember approximately zero of them.

**The exception:** two-part structures (paradox, contrast, before/after) are also memorable. "We thought we were building a product. We were actually building a movement." Two parts work when the tension between them is the point.

## Speech Writing Checklist

Run this before finalizing any speech:

- [ ] First 30 seconds contain no throat-clears, bio recitation, or agenda preview
- [ ] Core idea can be stated in one sentence without conjunctions
- [ ] At least one story per three minutes of speech
- [ ] Every piece of data is attached to a human story or consequence
- [ ] No more than three top-level points
- [ ] Pauses are marked in the script at key moments
- [ ] The close was written before the middle
- [ ] Opening and closing form a loop (callback present)
- [ ] Call to action is specific enough to act on immediately
- [ ] Ethos established in first 90 seconds
