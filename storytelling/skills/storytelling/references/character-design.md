# Character Design

> Characters are not lists of traits. They are defined by the gap between what they want and what they need. This file covers the structural principles that produce characters capable of driving a story — as distinct from characters who merely inhabit one.

---

## Want vs. need

The most productive distinction in character construction.

**Want:** The conscious, stated goal. What the character is actively pursuing. The engine of plot.

**Need:** The unconscious lack. What the character actually requires to become whole. The engine of arc.

The gap between want and need is where drama lives. A character who wants and needs the same thing has no arc — only a quest. A character whose want directly conflicts with their need has structural tension built in from the first scene.

**Examples:**

| Character | Want | Need |
|---|---|---|
| Luke Skywalker | To fight the Empire, to be a hero | To reconcile with his father; to become the Jedi his lineage could not produce |
| Michael Scott (The Office) | To be loved and admired by his employees | To stop looking for approval from others to feel valid |
| Walter White (Breaking Bad) | Ostensibly: money for his family | To admit that he is doing this for himself, for pride |
| Elizabeth Bennet (Pride and Prejudice) | To marry for love, not convenience | To let go of her first impressions and see people as they are |

In each case, the story is about the character's journey toward (or away from) the need — often while pursuing the want. The want gives the audience a concrete goal to follow; the need gives them a reason to care.

## The flaw

Every protagonist requires a flaw that is directly connected to what they need. A flaw that is unrelated to the need produces incoherence: the character overcomes something, but it does not resolve the central tension.

**Structural requirement:** The flaw must be what prevents the character from achieving the need. It is not a character quirk. It is not a weakness that is overcome in a side plot. It is the specific thing standing between the character and growth.

**The flaw-need connection:**

| Flaw | Need |
|---|---|
| Pride / inability to admit error | To accept vulnerability |
| Cowardice masked as pragmatism | To act on conviction regardless of personal cost |
| Emotional unavailability | To let someone close enough to matter |
| Control / inability to trust | To accept that others are capable and necessary |

**Common mistake:** Giving a protagonist a flaw that is actually an asset (she is "too dedicated" to her work, "too honest"). Audience members recognize a false flaw immediately. A real flaw costs the character something they do not want to lose.

## Three character arc types

K.M. Weiland's taxonomy (from *Creating Character Arcs*) is the most practically useful three-part classification.

### Positive change arc

The protagonist has a flaw, faces events that force confrontation with it, and ultimately overcomes it. The most common arc in Western narrative.

Structure: The protagonist holds a lie (a false belief about themselves or the world). The story's events expose the lie. The character either accepts the truth — and changes — or rejects it (see negative arc below).

### Flat arc

The protagonist does not change; the world around them does. Common in genre fiction (action, thriller, procedural) and in series where character consistency is a commercial requirement.

The flat arc protagonist holds a truth that the story's world has forgotten or rejected. By the end, the world is changed by contact with the protagonist's unwavering position.

Example: James Bond, Sherlock Holmes, many action heroes. The character's certainty is a feature, not a bug — the audience returns because the character is reliable.

**Structural note:** A flat arc requires that the secondary characters change. If no one changes, there is no arc at all — only event.

### Negative arc

The protagonist's flaw is not overcome but consumes them. Three varieties: the disillusionment arc (protagonist learns a hard but necessary truth and loses innocence), the fall arc (protagonist descends into moral failure), and the corruption arc (protagonist chooses the lie over the truth, with tragic consequences).

Examples: Macbeth (fall), Walter White (corruption), Holden Caulfield (disillusionment).

The negative arc is not less sophisticated or less satisfying than the positive arc. It is simply less common and requires that the story have a clear philosophical stance on what the character lost.

## Internal vs. external conflict

**External conflict** is the opposition between the protagonist and the world: antagonist, environment, circumstance. It opposes the want.

**Internal conflict** is the opposition within the protagonist: between the flaw and the need, between who they are and who they could be. It opposes the need.

The best stories design these conflicts to reinforce each other. The external conflict creates the conditions that force the internal conflict into the open. If these can be separated — if the external conflict could be resolved without engaging the internal conflict — the story has a structural problem.

**Example:** In *Ordinary People* (Judith Guest), Conrad's external conflict (navigating his family and school after his brother's death) cannot be resolved without his internal conflict (survivor's guilt, inability to grieve). Resolving one requires resolving the other. The two conflicts are locked.

## Dimensionality

E.M. Forster's distinction (*Aspects of the Novel*, 1927) between flat and round characters remains the most useful vocabulary for discussing character complexity.

**Flat characters** are defined by a single trait or function. They do not surprise. They serve plot.

**Round characters** contain contradictions. They act against their apparent type at least once in a way that feels earned rather than arbitrary. They surprise us — but in retrospect the surprise was inevitable.

Contradiction is the engine of dimensionality. A character who is generous but vindictive to those who embarrass them is more alive than a character who is simply generous. The contradiction does not need explanation — it just needs to be consistently present.

**Practical test:** List the five most prominent traits of your protagonist. Now ask: in what circumstances would this character act against each of those traits? If there is no convincing answer for any of them, the character is flat.

## The Save the Cat moment

Blake Snyder named this principle in *Save the Cat!* (2005): within the first ten minutes of a film (or pages of a script), the protagonist does something that makes the audience root for them — regardless of what morally complicated things follow.

The technique is especially critical when the protagonist is:
- Morally compromised from the opening
- Passive rather than active (which slows audience investment)
- Initially unlikeable or difficult to identify with

The moment does not need to be sentimental. It can be competence (we root for skilled people), wit (we like people who are funny at their own expense), or justice (we root for people who stand up to something unfair). The save-the-cat moment establishes the goodwill the story will spend on everything that follows.

## Supporting cast roles

Christopher Vogler's adaptation of Jung's archetypes gives each supporting character a structural function. A character can embody multiple archetypes or shift between them.

| Archetype | Function | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Mentor** | Provides guidance, tools, or wisdom; often sacrificed to propel the hero | Obi-Wan Kenobi, Gandalf, Alfred |
| **Ally** | Accompanies the hero, provides help and contrast | Samwise Gamgee, Donkey |
| **Herald** | Announces change; often the catalyst for the call to adventure | The letter, the messenger, the inciting character |
| **Shadow** | Embodies the hero's repressed qualities; the dark mirror | Darth Vader, Iago, Hans Landa |
| **Trickster** | Creates chaos; challenges the status quo; often comic relief with a deeper function | Loki, Q (James Bond), Tyler Durden |
| **Threshold Guardian** | Tests the hero before they can pass to the next stage | The gatekeepers, bureaucrats, skeptics |
| **Shapeshifter** | Changes allegiance or identity; creates doubt | The femme fatale, the double agent, the unreliable friend |

**Note:** These are functions, not character types. A mentor who is also a shapeshifter (appears to guide, but has their own agenda) is more interesting than a mentor who is purely supportive. Combining archetypes creates secondary characters with their own dimensionality.

## Character voice

Voice is not style or diction alone. Voice is the gap between how a character sees themselves and how the world — and the audience — sees them.

A character who believes they are magnanimous but is experienced as manipulative has a voice gap that generates dramatic irony and comedy. A character who believes they are weak but is experienced as formidable has a gap that generates tragedy and tension.

**In dialogue:** Voice reveals itself through what the character does not say. What a character says tells you who they want to be seen as; what they avoid, deflect from, or talk around tells you who they actually are.

**Practical technique:** Write a scene in which two characters want opposite things from the same conversation. Do not let either character directly state what they want. Force every line to pursue the want obliquely. The oblique lines will reveal character more than direct statements ever can.

## Antagonist construction

A weak antagonist destroys the story. The strength of the antagonist determines the ceiling of the story's dramatic potential.

The antagonist should be, functionally, the protagonist's shadow: the version of the protagonist who made different choices, holds different beliefs, or pursues the same want by opposite means. If the antagonist could be replaced by a natural disaster without changing the story, the antagonist is not doing their job.

**The antagonist's three requirements:**
1. They must have a coherent worldview — one that is internally logical even if morally wrong
2. They must have a want that directly conflicts with the protagonist's want
3. They must be capable of winning — at least until the climax

An antagonist who exists only to be defeated is an obstacle. An antagonist who believes they are right, who nearly succeeds, and whose defeat costs the protagonist something is a character.
