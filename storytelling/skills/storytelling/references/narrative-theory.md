## Vladimir Propp's 31 Functions

From *Morphology of the Folktale* (1928; English translation 1958). Propp analyzed a corpus of Russian folk tales and found that all of them use the same 31 narrative events (functions) in the same order. Not every tale contains all 31, but no tale violates the sequence.

The claim is not that all stories are the same. It is that folk tales share a deep grammar — the same events, ordered the same way, regardless of surface variation in characters, settings, or names.

**The 31 functions in order:**

| # | Name | Description |
|---|------|-------------|
| 1 | Initial situation | The hero and family are introduced |
| 2 | Absentation | A family member leaves or is absent |
| 3 | Interdiction | A prohibition or rule is given to the hero |
| 4 | Violation | The interdiction is violated |
| 5 | Reconnaissance | The villain seeks information |
| 6 | Delivery | The villain receives information |
| 7 | Trickery | The villain deceives the hero or family |
| 8 | Complicity | The victim is deceived |
| 9 | Villainy / Lack | The villain causes harm, or a lack is established |
| 10 | Mediation | The hero learns of the villainy or lack |
| 11 | Counteraction | The hero decides to act |
| 12 | Departure | The hero leaves home |
| 13 | First donor function | The hero is tested by a donor figure |
| 14 | Hero's reaction | The hero passes or fails the donor's test |
| 15 | Receipt of magical agent | The hero receives a helper or magical object |
| 16 | Guidance | The hero is led to the object of the quest |
| 17 | Struggle | The hero and villain engage in direct conflict |
| 18 | Branding | The hero is marked (wounded, branded, given a token) |
| 19 | Victory | The villain is defeated |
| 20 | Liquidation | The initial lack or misfortune is resolved |
| 21 | Return | The hero begins the journey home |
| 22 | Pursuit | The hero is chased |
| 23 | Rescue | The hero escapes pursuit |
| 24 | Unrecognized arrival | The hero returns unrecognized |
| 25 | Unfounded claims | A false hero makes a claim to the prize |
| 26 | Difficult task | The hero is given a final test |
| 27 | Solution | The hero passes the test |
| 28 | Recognition | The hero is identified |
| 29 | Exposure | The false hero is unmasked |
| 30 | Transfiguration | The hero is transformed (new appearance, status) |
| 31 | Punishment | The villain is punished |
| 32 | Wedding | The hero marries and ascends (Propp added this as a coda) |

**Practical use:** Propp's functions are more useful as a diagnostic tool than a template. When a folk-derived narrative (fairy tale adaptation, hero myth, classic quest) feels structurally incomplete, checking which functions are absent often identifies the gap. The framework is less applicable to psychological realism, literary fiction, or non-linear narrative.

## Georges Polti's 36 Dramatic Situations

From *The Thirty-Six Dramatic Situations* (1895; English translation 1916). Polti claimed that every possible dramatic situation in literature could be reduced to 36 archetypal conflicts. The claim has never been proven or disproven — it is more useful as a taxonomy than a theory.

**The 36 situations:**

1. Supplication
2. Deliverance
3. Crime pursued by vengeance
4. Vengeance taken for kindred upon kindred
5. Pursuit
6. Disaster
7. Falling prey to cruelty or misfortune
8. Revolt
9. Daring enterprise
10. Abduction
11. The enigma (riddle, puzzle, mystery)
12. Obtaining (something through effort or cunning)
13. Enmity of kin
14. Rivalry of kin
15. Murderous adultery
16. Madness
17. Fatal imprudence
18. Involuntary crimes of love
19. Slaying of a kinsman unrecognized
20. Self-sacrifice for an ideal
21. Self-sacrifice for kindred
22. All sacrificed for a passion
23. Necessity of sacrificing loved ones
24. Rivalry of superior and inferior
25. Adultery
26. Crimes of love
27. Discovery of the dishonor of a loved one
28. Obstacles to love
29. An enemy loved
30. Ambition
31. Conflict with a god
32. Mistaken jealousy
33. Erroneous judgment
34. Remorse
35. Recovery of a lost one
36. Loss of loved ones

**Notes on the list:** Polti himself acknowledged significant overlap between situations — his divisions are sometimes arbitrary. The value is not the exact list but the act of categorization: recognizing that your story's conflict belongs to a known type and asking how the tradition has handled it. Situations 1–10 are most commonly cited; the later situations blend into each other.

## Christopher Booker's Seven Basic Plots

From *The Seven Basic Plots: Why We Tell Stories* (2004, after 34 years of writing). Booker's claim: every story in every culture is one of seven archetypal patterns. This is more reductive than Propp or Polti and more controversial, but the seven categories are memorable and practically useful for identifying what type of story you are telling.

| Plot | Core pattern | Classic examples |
|------|-------------|-----------------|
| **Overcoming the Monster** | Hero faces and defeats an external, destructive force | *Beowulf*, *Jaws*, *Star Wars* |
| **Rags to Riches** | Humble protagonist rises through talent or virtue | *Cinderella*, *David Copperfield*, *Aladdin* |
| **The Quest** | Hero and companions travel to obtain an object or reach a destination | *Lord of the Rings*, *Raiders of the Lost Ark*, *The Odyssey* |
| **Voyage and Return** | Protagonist enters a strange world and returns changed | *Alice in Wonderland*, *The Wizard of Oz*, *Narnia* |
| **Comedy** | Light treatment of human confusion and folly, resolved happily | Shakespeare's comedies, romantic comedies |
| **Tragedy** | Protagonist's fatal flaw leads to downfall | *Macbeth*, *Anna Karenina*, *Breaking Bad* |
| **Rebirth** | Dark force dominates until protagonist is redeemed or awakened | *A Christmas Carol*, *Beauty and the Beast*, *The Secret Garden* |

**The controversy:** Critics note that Booker's framework is deeply Western and Indo-European in its cultural assumptions. Narratives from East Asian, African, and Indigenous traditions do not map cleanly onto these categories. The framework is also so broad that many stories fit multiple plots simultaneously. Booker himself is at times more prescriptive than descriptive — arguing that stories deviating from these patterns are flawed.

**Practical use:** The seven plots are most useful for genre recognition and reader expectation management. Knowing that a reader picks up a "Quest" story with specific expectations allows a writer to either satisfy or subvert them deliberately. Unintentional subversion — delivering a Tragedy when readers expected a Voyage and Return — explains many audience mismatches.

## Ronald Tobias's 20 Master Plots

From *20 Master Plots (And How to Build Them)* (1993). A middle-ground taxonomy: more granular than Booker's seven, more memorable than Polti's 36. Tobias focuses on what drives the story forward (the master plot) rather than the outcome.

**The 20 plots:**

| Plot | Central drive |
|------|--------------|
| Quest | Seeking an object, place, or person |
| Adventure | A series of events in an exotic or dangerous world |
| Pursuit | One party chasing another |
| Rescue | Freeing someone from captivity or danger |
| Escape | Breaking free from confinement |
| Revenge | Retribution for a wrong |
| The Riddle | Solving a mystery or puzzle |
| Rivalry | Competition between equals |
| Underdog | Overcoming systemic disadvantage |
| Temptation | Resisting or succumbing to forbidden desire |
| Metamorphosis | Physical transformation with psychological consequence |
| Transformation | Internal change in character or worldview |
| Maturation | Coming-of-age, learning to be an adult |
| Love | Union against obstacles |
| Forbidden Love | Love that violates social or moral codes |
| Sacrifice | Giving up something precious for a higher value |
| Discovery | Finding out a life-altering truth |
| Wretched Excess | Hubris and its consequences |
| Ascension | Rise to power or status |
| Descension | Fall from power or status |

**The distinction between Metamorphosis and Transformation:** Metamorphosis is physical change with psychological resonance (*The Fly*, Kafka's *Metamorphosis*). Transformation is purely internal change (*A Christmas Carol* without the supernatural, most literary fiction).

**Practical use:** When a story feels unfocused, identifying the master plot clarifies what every scene should be doing. A Rescue plot's scenes should all be advancing or complicating the rescue. A scene that is neither doing that nor showing us character relevant to the rescue may not belong.

## Joseph Campbell's Monomyth

From *The Hero with a Thousand Faces* (1949). Campbell analyzed myths from dozens of cultures and proposed that all myths share a single deep structure: the monomyth, or Hero's Journey. The claim is among the most influential — and most contested — in narrative theory.

**Campbell's 17 stages** (organized into three phases):

**Departure**
1. The ordinary world (the call to adventure)
2. The call to adventure
3. Refusal of the call
4. Acceptance of supernatural aid
5. Crossing the first threshold
6. The belly of the whale (full commitment to the journey)

**Initiation**
7. The road of trials
8. Meeting with the goddess (or unconditional love)
9. Woman as temptress (or distraction from the quest)
10. Atonement with the father
11. Apotheosis
12. The ultimate boon

**Return**
13. Refusal of the return
14. The magic flight
15. Rescue from without
16. The crossing of the return threshold
17. Master of two worlds
18. Freedom to live

**Christopher Vogler's 12-stage simplification** (from *The Writer's Journey*, 1992) reduced Campbell's schema for screenwriters and became the template taught in most Hollywood development circles:

1. Ordinary world
2. Call to adventure
3. Refusal of the call
4. Meeting the mentor
5. Crossing the threshold
6. Tests, allies, and enemies
7. Approach to the inmost cave
8. The ordeal
9. Reward (seizing the sword)
10. The road back
11. Resurrection
12. Return with the elixir

**Famous examples:** *Star Wars* (the structural alignment is unusually close), *The Matrix*, *The Lion King*, virtually every Disney feature from 1989–2005.

**The critique:** Campbell's universality claim has been challenged by scholars of comparative mythology (Doniger, Segal, Dundes). The monomyth is most clearly visible in Western, Indo-European, and East Asian hero myths. Trickster narratives, tragedy, lyric narrative, and many non-Western traditions do not fit the pattern. The framework also centers a specifically masculine hero and a specific (Western) concept of individuation.

**The practical defense:** Even if the monomyth is not truly universal, the Vogler schema maps the audience's emotional journey in a way that produces recognizable narrative satisfaction. It describes, at minimum, what Western audiences raised on Hollywood film have been trained to expect.

## Gérard Genette's Narratology

From *Narrative Discourse* (1972; English translation 1980). Genette provided the most systematic vocabulary for analyzing *how* stories are told, as distinct from *what* they contain. This is indispensable for close analysis and for explaining why a technically competent story still fails.

**Core distinctions:**

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Story (histoire)** | The events in their logical, chronological order |
| **Discourse (récit)** | The events as narrated — the text, with its sequence and emphasis |
| **Narration** | The act of telling, and who is doing it |

A story's events may be told out of order (discourse), by a narrator who was not present (narration), while the underlying events have a fixed chronology (story). These three levels are independently variable.

**Order** — how the discourse relates to the story's chronology:
- *Analepsis* (flashback): narrating a past event after later events
- *Prolepsis* (flash-forward): anticipating a future event before its chronological place

**Duration** — how much text is devoted to how much story time:
- *Scene*: text time ≈ story time (fully dramatized)
- *Summary*: text compresses many story events into brief narration
- *Ellipsis*: story time passes with no text (time jump)
- *Pause*: text time without story time (description, reflection)
- *Stretch*: text time > story time (slow-motion, interior monologue)

**Frequency** — how often an event is narrated relative to how often it occurred:
- *Singulative*: one narration of one occurrence (the default)
- *Repetitive*: multiple narrations of one occurrence (told from different perspectives)
- *Iterative*: one narration covering many similar occurrences ("every morning she…")

**Voice** — who narrates:
- *Homodiegetic*: narrator is inside the story world (first person)
- *Heterodiegetic*: narrator is outside the story world (third person omniscient)
- *Autodiegetic*: narrator is the protagonist

**Mood** — distance and perspective:
- *Focalization*: whose consciousness filters the narrative? (zero focalization = omniscient; internal = character's perspective; external = camera-eye)

**Practical use:** When a story's tone feels inconsistent, check for focalization shifts. When a story drags, check duration — likely too many scenes, not enough summary. When a story's timeline is confusing, map the analepses and prolepses explicitly.

## A.J. Greimas's Actantial Model

From *Structural Semantics* (1966). Greimas, following Propp and Lévi-Strauss, proposed that all narratives can be reduced to six roles (actants) and three axes of relation.

**The six actants:**

| Actant | Role |
|--------|------|
| **Subject** | Seeks something |
| **Object** | What is sought |
| **Sender** | Who sets the quest or motivates the subject |
| **Receiver** | Who benefits from the quest's success |
| **Helper** | Who assists the subject |
| **Opponent** | Who hinders the subject |

**The three axes:**

| Axis | Pair |
|------|------|
| Desire | Subject ↔ Object |
| Communication | Sender ↔ Receiver |
| Power | Helper ↔ Opponent |

**Example — *Star Wars* (1977):**

| Actant | Character |
|--------|-----------|
| Subject | Luke Skywalker |
| Object | Destroy the Death Star / restore peace |
| Sender | Princess Leia (the distress message); the Force |
| Receiver | The Rebellion; the galaxy |
| Helpers | Han Solo, Obi-Wan Kenobi, R2-D2, the Force |
| Opponent | Darth Vader, the Empire, the Death Star itself |

**Note:** Actants are roles, not characters. One character can fill multiple actant roles (Obi-Wan is both Helper and Sender). A single actant role can be filled by multiple characters (all Rebel pilots are Helpers).

**Practical use:** When a story feels structurally weak, mapping the actants often reveals what is missing. A story without a clear Sender has no justification for the quest. A story where the Subject and Object are misaligned (the protagonist wants one thing but the story is about something else) explains a common type of unfocused narrative.

## How to Use Theory Practically

Narrative theory describes. Craft prescribes. Both are necessary; neither is sufficient alone.

**Theory is most useful for:**
- Diagnosing structural problems that craft intuition has not resolved
- Teaching narrative to others (theory provides shared vocabulary)
- Analyzing existing work to understand what it is doing and why
- Recognizing genre expectations and how to meet or subvert them

**Theory is least useful for:**
- First drafts (theory applied during drafting produces mechanical stories)
- Moments when the story is working but does not fit a known pattern
- Forms that the theorists did not study (most Western theory is built on a narrow corpus)

**Practical protocol for using theory diagnostically:**

1. Identify the specific problem: "The ending feels unearned." "The protagonist feels passive." "The middle sags."
2. Select the most relevant framework:
   - Ending unearned → check Propp (is a function missing?), check McKee's obligatory scene, check deus ex machina
   - Protagonist passive → check Greimas (is the Subject clearly defined? Is the Object clear?), check Campbell (is the protagonist refusing the call too long?)
   - Middle sags → check Genette (is duration wrong? Too much scene, not enough summary?), check three-act structure for midpoint
3. Apply the framework as a diagnostic, not a template.
4. Fix the production code (the story), not the test (the theory).

## Narrative Theory vs. Craft

| Dimension | Theory | Craft |
|-----------|--------|-------|
| Mode | Descriptive — what stories do |  Prescriptive — what stories should do |
| Method | Analysis of existing texts | Practice and revision |
| Risk | Academic without application | Mechanical without theory |
| Primary question | How does this work? | How do I make this work? |

Both are required for serious practitioners. Theory without craft is an analyst who cannot write. Craft without theory is a practitioner who cannot explain their choices or diagnose their failures. The goal is fluency in both registers.
