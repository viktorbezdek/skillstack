> Bad dialogue is the fastest way to lose a reader. Good dialogue is never what the character means — it is what they are willing to say. This reference covers the craft mechanics of written dialogue: subtext, voice, attribution, pace, and the silence between lines.

---

## Text vs. Subtext

The text is what is said. The subtext is what is meant. Great dialogue carries more subtext than text.

| Text | Subtext | Why it works |
|------|---------|--------------|
| "I love you." | I love you. | No gap — flat and functional for a declaration scene. |
| "Did you eat?" | I care about you and I'm worried. | Ordinary words carry emotional freight. |
| "The dishes are still in the sink." | You never respect my time or effort. | Complaint about a relationship dressed as domestic observation. |
| "Nice car." | I resent your success. | Compliment that is transparently not a compliment. |

**Rule:** If the text and the subtext are identical, you probably don't need dialogue at all. Summarize in narration and move on.

**Test:** Read the line aloud. Ask: could this same sentence mean the opposite in a different context? If yes, you have subtext. If the sentence can only ever mean exactly what it says, it's on-the-nose.

## Voice Differentiation

If you cover the dialogue tags and can still identify who is speaking, the voices are differentiated. If every character sounds like the author, voices are not differentiated.

**Diagnostic:** Print a scene. Black out every tag and action beat. Read the dialogue lines. Can you assign them to characters? If not, rewrite.

**Dimensions to differentiate on:**

| Dimension | Technique |
|-----------|-----------|
| Vocabulary | College professor uses latinate words; construction worker uses concrete nouns and Anglo-Saxon verbs. |
| Sentence length | Anxious characters speak in fragments. Deliberate characters finish their thoughts. |
| Formality | Register shifts with power dynamics — a character talks differently to their boss than to their child. |
| What they avoid | A coward never names their fear directly. A liar avoids the specific. |
| Rhythm | Some characters build to a point; others lead with it. Some interrupt; some wait. |
| Filler and tics | "Look" as a sentence opener. "You know what I mean?" as a closer. Use sparingly — once or twice establishes the habit; more becomes parody. |

**The author-voice problem:** Characters built from the same worldview as the author will share vocabulary, metaphors, and argument structure. The fix is not imitation — it is getting inside the character's actual situation until their way of seeing the world differs from yours.

## The "As You Know, Bob" Problem

Named after the clunky move of having one character explain something to another character who already knows it — solely so the reader learns it.

> "As you know, Bob, the reactor core runs on deuterium and requires a 72-hour cooldown cycle after each use."

Bob knows this. He has worked here for eleven years. The writer needed the reader to know it.

**Why it fails:** It breaks the fictional contract. Characters have no reason to say this to this person in this moment. Readers feel the puppet strings.

**Fixes:**

1. **Find a genuine knowledge gap.** Have a new character, a confused character, or someone outside the situation ask the question. The explanation is now motivated.
2. **Move it to narration.** Third-person narration can deliver exposition without pretending a character is speaking it.
3. **Let the reader infer.** Often the reader does not need to know the technical detail at all. Trust the inference.
4. **Dramatize the gap.** If two characters disagree about how the reactor works, the argument delivers the exposition through conflict.

## On-the-Nose Dialogue

Characters state exactly what they feel. "I'm angry at you for leaving." "I feel guilty about what I did." "I'm afraid of the dark."

Real people almost never speak this way. Emotion is expressed obliquely, through misdirection, silence, displacement, and indirection. Flat emotional statements drain tension because they leave nothing for the reader to feel — the text has already done the work the reader wanted to do.

**Fixes:**

- **Add an obstacle.** The character cannot say what they mean because saying it would cost them something. What is the cost? Let that cost shape the words they choose instead.
- **Add misdirection.** The character says something adjacent — a related topic, a smaller grievance, the surface complaint — while the real feeling runs underneath.
- **Compress.** "I'm angry at you for leaving" becomes a look, a monosyllable, a refusal to sit down, or nothing at all. The anger is structural, not stated.
- **Displace onto object or action.** The angry character fidgets with their keys, or speaks to the dog, or opens the refrigerator and stares into it.

## Interiority

Interiority is the character's thoughts and sensory experience between and during lines of dialogue. It is the interpretive layer: what the POV character makes of what is being said.

**Too little interiority** makes dialogue feel like a screenplay — functional but cold. The reader processes words without inhabiting a consciousness.

**Too much interiority** over-explains the scene. If the character thinks "She realized he was lying," the subtext has been named and defused.

**The rule:** Interiority should reveal what dialogue conceals, not annotate what dialogue already shows.

**Calibration guide:**

| Situation | Interiority level |
|-----------|-------------------|
| Rapid argument | Minimal — short sharp beats, or none |
| Character receiving bad news | Deep — the gap between hearing and responding |
| Small talk with hidden stakes | Moderate — shows the character monitoring, calculating |
| Emotional confrontation | Moderate — shows suppression, not the emotion itself |

## Attribution

"Said" is invisible. Readers process it as punctuation. Use it freely.

Fancy tags — "he exclaimed," "she protested," "he ejaculated," "she hissed" — pull attention to themselves. They announce craft rather than deploying it.

**Adverbs on tags** ("she said softly," "he said angrily") are doubly damaging: they tell the reader what tone to hear rather than building it into the words. If the line reads as angry, the adverb is redundant. If it doesn't, the adverb is insufficient — rewrite the line.

**Action beats as attribution:**

> She set the cup down. "I'm not going."

The action beat replaces the tag entirely, adds a physical dimension to the scene, and characterizes (deliberate action before a defiant statement). This is the preferred form for emotionally charged lines.

**When "said" is not enough:**

- Use "asked" for genuine questions.
- Use "whispered" or "called" when volume is narratively meaningful.
- Beyond these, use action beats, not fancy tags.

## Dialect and Accent

Handle with extreme care.

Heavy phonetic spelling — "wot yer doin' 'ere, guv?" — alienates readers, slows pace, and can be offensive when applied selectively (only dialect-speaking characters get this treatment; standard-American characters get clean prose).

**Preferred techniques:**

- **Word choice:** A character who says "reckon," "ain't," and "y'all" reads as regional without a single phonetic distortion.
- **Rhythm:** Sentence structure encodes speech patterns. Inverted syntax, dropped subjects, run-ons.
- **A few marker phrases:** One or two recognizable constructions establish the pattern. The reader's imagination fills in the rest.
- **Consistency:** Dialect markers that appear and disappear feel like costume, not voice.

## Silence as Dialogue

What a character does not say is data. The pause, the refusal to answer, the subject changed at exactly the wrong moment — these are as structural as spoken lines.

**Forms of silence:**

| Form | What it signals |
|------|----------------|
| Pause before answering | Processing, reluctance, calculation |
| Non-answer | The question is unanswerable, or the answer is unacceptable |
| Subject change | The topic is dangerous; redirect |
| Agreement that doesn't convince | The character acquiesces verbally but resists emotionally |
| No response | Contempt, shock, or the choice to end the exchange |

Silence works because it forces the reader to supply meaning. The reader becomes active. That activity produces investment.

## Pace in Dialogue

Short exchanges accelerate. Longer speeches decelerate. Mix them.

**Acceleration techniques:**

- Remove all attribution; let the lines fire without interruption.
- Cut speeches to single sentences or fragments.
- End each line with a pressure that forces the next speaker to respond.

**Deceleration techniques:**

- Interrupt dialogue with action beats or interiority.
- Let a character speak for a longer passage — a story, an explanation, a monologue.
- Insert a pause (handled as interiority or action, not as a tag).

A scene that runs at the same pace throughout loses tension. Even a high-stakes argument benefits from a moment that slows — the character who has to collect themselves before they can answer back.

## The Three-Beat Rule

Most dialogue exchanges benefit from three beats: setup, misdirection, payoff.

- **One beat:** Terse. Can be effective for emphasis but reads as incomplete for ordinary exchanges.
- **Two beats:** Common, but often feels like a setup without resolution.
- **Three beats:** The minimum complete unit. Setup establishes context; misdirection creates surprise or complication; payoff lands the actual meaning.
- **Four or more beats:** The exchange starts to feel padded unless each additional beat adds new information or changes the dynamic.

**Example:**

> "You could have called." *(setup)*
> "I lost my phone." *(misdirection — not an apology)*
> "You knew where I lived." *(payoff — the real charge)*

The third beat reframes everything before it. That reframing is the work.

## Quick-Reference Checklist

Before finalizing a dialogue scene:

- [ ] Can you identify who is speaking without reading the tags?
- [ ] Does each line have subtext, or is it only delivering text?
- [ ] Are there any "as you know, Bob" moments? Eliminate or justify them.
- [ ] Are attribution tags limited to "said," "asked," and volume words?
- [ ] Is interiority present but not over-explaining?
- [ ] Does pace vary across the scene?
- [ ] Have you read the dialogue aloud and cut anything that sounds like writing?
- [ ] Does any character say exactly what they feel? If yes, is that a deliberate craft choice?
