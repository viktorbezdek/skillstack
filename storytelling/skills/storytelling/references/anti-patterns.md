> Most failing stories are not missing something — they have something they should not. This file names the seven most common narrative anti-patterns, the signals that reveal them, and the concrete fixes. These apply across fiction, screenwriting, game narrative, and non-fiction storytelling.

---

## The Seven Anti-Patterns

---

### 1. Info Dump

**What it looks like**

Paragraphs of backstory, worldbuilding, or exposition dropped into the text, usually early, usually in dialogue or narration, with little relation to immediate narrative need.

> *"As Lord Alric rode through the capital, he remembered how the Kingdom of Elaria had been founded three hundred years ago by the warrior-mage Erindal, who had united the seven clans after the Sundering Wars. The capital itself had been built on the ruins of the old city, which had burned in the Year of Ash when the dragon Velakroth…"*

**Why it happens**

The writer has thought deeply about the world and wants the reader to have the same context. The backstory feels essential. It is not, yet.

**Why it destroys the story**

The reader is not invested yet. Without investment, information has nothing to attach to — it reads as homework. The reader skips or skims. Worse, the info dump signals that the writer trusts the world more than the story.

**Fix**

Cut 80%. Introduce world through what characters *need to do* in the present, not what they know about the past. Readers are capable of assembling context from fragments — trust them. The remaining 20% should be delivered in small doses, only when the reader needs the information to understand what just happened.

**Self-audit question**

Highlight every paragraph of pure description, summary, or history. For each: can it be converted to action, or cut entirely? If the answer to both is no, ask why the reader needs this now.

### 2. Mary Sue / Marty Stu

**What it looks like**

The protagonist is good at everything, makes no serious mistakes, is universally liked or respected, has no real flaw, and wins through competence rather than transformation.

Common symptoms:
- Other characters exist primarily to praise the protagonist
- The protagonist's one stated "flaw" is a strength (perfectionism, caring too much)
- Problems resolve because the protagonist is clever or talented, not because they changed
- The protagonist is never genuinely wrong

**Why it happens**

The writer loves the character and protects them. Often the character is a self-insertion figure, consciously or not.

**Why it destroys the story**

Without real stakes for the protagonist — without the possibility of genuine failure, shame, or loss — there is no arc. No arc means no story, only events. Readers identify with characters through their flaws, not their virtues.

**Fix**

Give the protagonist a flaw that is directly connected to what they need to learn by the end. The flaw must cost them something significant in act 1 or act 2 — not a minor inconvenience, but a genuine failure with lasting consequence. The resolution of this flaw is the arc.

**Self-audit question**

In which scene does the protagonist fail — not through bad luck, but because of their own flaw? If no such scene exists, the character needs one.

### 3. Deus Ex Machina

**What it looks like**

The climactic problem is solved by something that had not been established: a new character who appears and rescues everyone, a device with unexplained capabilities, a coincidence, a power the protagonist suddenly discovers they have.

The term comes from Greek theatre, where a machine lowered an actor playing a god onto the stage to resolve an unresolvable plot.

**Why it happens**

The writer wrote the protagonist into a corner and cannot find a way out that uses the story's established logic.

**Why it destroys the story**

The audience has been tracking what is possible within the story's rules. A resolution that violates those rules feels like cheating. Victory without earned cost is not victory — it is a gift from the author. The audience's investment was based on caring whether the protagonist could overcome the problem; if the problem is resolved by something outside the protagonist's agency, the investment was wasted.

**Fix**

Foreshadow the solution in act 1 or early act 2. Robert McKee's principle: the obligatory scene should feel *inevitable in retrospect* and *surprising in the moment*. If the climax depends on a specific object, ability, or person, plant that element early enough that the reader registers it but does not yet know it will matter.

**Self-audit question**

Can a careful reader, re-reading the story, find the moment where the solution was planted? If they cannot, it is deus ex machina.

### 4. "As You Know, Bob"

**What it looks like**

Two characters explain to each other information they both already know, for the reader's benefit.

> *"As you know, Bob, our company was founded in 2012 by my father, who was the CEO until his death last year — which is why this merger proposal is so complicated for our family."*

Neither character would say this to the other. The writer is using them as puppets.

**Why it happens**

The writer needs to convey exposition and cannot find a narrative vehicle for it. Dialogue feels less intrusive than narration, but the illusion fails immediately.

**Why it destroys the story**

It breaks suspension of disbelief. The moment a reader notices a character saying something purely for the reader's benefit, the fictional frame cracks. Both characters become visible as constructs rather than people.

**Fix**

Three alternatives:
1. **Find a character who does not know.** A new employee, an outsider, a child. Their ignorance gives the informed character a genuine reason to explain.
2. **Move it to narration.** Omniscient narration can carry exposition without the fakery of dialogue. Third-person past tense: "The company had been founded in 2012, in the year before his father's death."
3. **Let the reader figure it out from context.** Often the reader does not need the information as explicitly as the writer fears.

**Self-audit question**

Would either character actually say this to the other in a real conversation? If not, rewrite.

### 5. Telling, Not Showing

**What it looks like**

The narration summarizes rather than dramatizes the emotionally important moments.

> *"She was devastated when she learned her father had died."*

vs.

> *"The call lasted forty seconds. Afterward she stood in the kitchen holding the phone until the screen went dark, then set it on the counter, and sat down on the floor."*

**Why it happens**

Summarizing is faster. The writer wants to get to the next scene. Emotional beats feel self-evident — why describe the grief when you can just name it?

**Why it destroys the story**

The reader cannot feel what a character feels unless they witness it. Naming an emotion tells the reader what to feel. Dramatizing an emotion lets the reader feel it themselves. The emotional climax of a story that is told rather than shown has no weight; the reader understands it intellectually but is not moved.

**Critical caveat:** Telling is appropriate — even necessary — for transitions, minor events, time compression, and logistics. "Three days passed." "She drove home." Not everything needs a scene. The anti-pattern applies specifically to *emotionally important moments*, which must be dramatized.

**Fix**

At each major emotional beat, write the scene rather than the summary. Show the moment being delivered, the body language, the small specific detail. Cut the adjective that names the emotion; trust the concrete detail to carry it.

**Self-audit question**

At each major emotional beat in the story: is there a scene or a summary? If it is a summary, ask whether this beat is important enough to deserve a scene. If yes, write the scene.

### 6. Moral Misalignment

**What it looks like**

The story's outcome does not match the moral premise it established.

- A redemption arc that ends without the character being redeemed
- A story set up as "honesty is costly but necessary" where the dishonest characters prosper without consequence
- A protagonist who repeatedly cheats and wins, in a story that framed cheating as the central conflict

**Why it happens**

The writer lost track of what the story is actually about, or changed direction mid-draft without rethinking the premise.

**Why it destroys the story**

The audience has been making a bet on the story's moral logic. If the ending does not pay off that logic — if there is no earned consequence, no resolution of the central question — the audience feels cheated or confused. The story feels hollow. Not because it lacks a happy ending, but because it lacks a *coherent* one.

**Fix**

Lajos Egri's concept: every story has a *premise* — a one-sentence statement of the story's argument. "Ambition without conscience leads to destruction." "Loyalty to the dead prevents love of the living." The premise is not the plot; it is the point. Every major plot event should complicate or advance the premise, and the ending must resolve it definitively.

Write your premise before drafting. When in doubt about whether a scene belongs, test it against the premise.

**Self-audit question**

State the story's point in one sentence. Does the ending deliver a clear verdict on that point? If not, find the misalignment and fix the ending or revise the premise.

### 7. Manufactured Conflict

**What it looks like**

Characters are in conflict about something that could be resolved by one honest conversation. The plot depends on characters being unusually uncommunicative, failing to share obvious information, or making decisions no rational person would make.

> *"If she had just told him what she saw, none of this would have happened."*

This thought — common in poorly constructed thrillers and romantic dramas — is the reader's signal that the conflict is manufactured.

**Why it happens**

Conflict is essential; generating real conflict is hard. The easiest form of conflict is withheld information. The writer reaches for it without building in a credible reason for the withholding.

**Why it destroys the story**

The reader loses respect for the characters. Watching people fail to communicate simple information while catastrophe unfolds is not dramatic — it is frustrating. The reader feels manipulated, not engaged.

**Fix**

Real conflict is structural, not communicative. Money, love, death, freedom, identity — these cannot be resolved by one honest conversation. If your conflict *can* be resolved by one honest conversation, find the deeper conflict underneath it.

When information must be withheld for plot reasons, the reason for withholding must be rooted in the character's defining flaw, history, or fear — something that is itself part of the arc. The character does not tell the truth because the truth is bound up in shame that is their central wound. That withholding is now characterization, not plot convenience.

**Self-audit question**

Can the conflict be resolved by one honest conversation between the parties involved? If yes, the conflict is manufactured. Find a conflict that cannot be resolved by conversation.

## Quick Audit Checklist

Run every draft past these seven questions before publishing or sharing:

| # | Question |
|---|---------|
| 1 | Is there any paragraph of pure exposition that could be converted to action or cut? |
| 2 | In which scene does the protagonist fail because of their own flaw? |
| 3 | Can a careful reader find where the climactic solution was planted? |
| 4 | Does any character explain to another character something they both know? |
| 5 | At each major emotional beat: is there a scene or a summary? |
| 6 | State the story's premise in one sentence. Does the ending resolve it? |
| 7 | Can the central conflict be resolved by one honest conversation? |

A "no" on questions 2, 3, 6 or a "yes" on questions 1, 4, 7 indicates a structural problem that revision must address before the story is ready.
