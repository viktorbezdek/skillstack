# Story Structures

> There is no single correct structure. Structures are tools; match the tool to the story and the audience. This file covers nine major structural frameworks, a comparison table, and a decision guide for choosing between them.

---

## Freytag's Pyramid

Developed by Gustav Freytag in *Technique of the Drama* (1863) as a descriptive analysis of Greek and Shakespearean drama, not a prescriptive template. Still the default model in academic and literary contexts.

**Five stages:**
1. **Exposition** — introduce characters, setting, and the situation before conflict begins
2. **Rising action** — a series of events that build tension and complicate the situation
3. **Climax** — the turning point; the moment of maximum tension and irreversibility
4. **Falling action** — consequences of the climax unfold; tension releases
5. **Denouement** — final resolution; the new equilibrium

**Practical notes:**
- The falling action and denouement are often compressed or omitted in modern storytelling — audiences tolerate less resolution than 19th-century theater audiences did.
- The model describes five-act plays well. Applying it mechanically to prose or short-form content produces stilted structure.
- Most useful as a diagnostic: if your climax is at the 90% mark and nothing follows, you are writing a Freytag pyramid with a vestigial denouement.

## Three-Act Structure

The dominant model in Hollywood screenwriting and the implicit default in most Western narrative-based communication. Described by Syd Field in *Screenplay* (1979) and refined by countless successors.

**Three acts:**
1. **Setup (Act 1, ~25%)** — establish the protagonist, their world, their want. Ends with the first plot point: an event that commits the protagonist to the central conflict.
2. **Confrontation (Act 2, ~50%)** — protagonist pursues the want, faces escalating obstacles. A midpoint (50% mark) shifts the action. Ends with the second plot point: the darkest moment, protagonist appears to fail.
3. **Resolution (Act 3, ~25%)** — protagonist overcomes (or fails to overcome) the central conflict. Climax. Brief denouement.

**Percentage markers for a 100-page screenplay (or 100-slide deck, or 60-minute talk):**
- Page 10–12: inciting incident (first disruption)
- Page 25: end of act 1 / first plot point
- Page 50: midpoint reversal
- Page 75: end of act 2 / second plot point / "all is lost"
- Page 85–90: climax
- Page 95–100: resolution

**Practical notes:**
- The model's strength is pacing predictability — experienced audiences calibrate to it.
- Its weakness is rigidity: stories that force themselves into 25/50/25 splits often have sagging middles or rushed climaxes.
- The midpoint is frequently ignored by beginners. Adding a midpoint reversal (a false victory or escalation) is the single most reliable fix for a saggy second act.

## Fichtean Curve

Less commonly named but widely practiced, especially in thriller and suspense writing. Named after Johann Gottlieb Fichte, though the connection to his philosophy is loose.

**Structure:**
- Story begins at or near the first crisis, with no extended setup
- A series of escalating crises propels the protagonist forward
- Each crisis is partially resolved but raises new stakes
- The climax is the largest crisis; the falling action is minimal or absent

**Key difference from Freytag:** No extended exposition. The audience learns who the protagonist is through action, not setup. This creates immediate tension but requires skilled backstory management (revelation through action rather than explanation).

**Best for:** Thrillers, mysteries, suspense narratives, action films, tightly paced marketing narratives where attention must be captured immediately.

## Hero's Journey

Joseph Campbell documented the *monomyth* in *The Hero with a Thousand Faces* (1949) across mythology from dozens of cultures. Christopher Vogler adapted it into a practical 12-stage screenwriting tool in *The Writer's Journey* (1992).

**Campbell's 17 stages** (often abbreviated):
1. The Ordinary World
2. The Call to Adventure
3. Refusal of the Call
4. Meeting the Mentor
5. Crossing the First Threshold
6. Tests, Allies, and Enemies
7. Approach to the Inmost Cave
8. The Ordeal
9. The Reward (Seizing the Sword)
10. The Road Back
11. The Resurrection
12. Return with the Elixir

(Campbell's full 17-stage version adds the supernatural aid and road of trials; Vogler's 12-stage version is the practical working model.)

**The critical insight:** The hero must return. A journey without a return to the ordinary world does not complete the mythic structure — the elixir, the lesson, the transformation must be brought back to the community it serves. This is why "hero goes on adventure and stays in the magical world" feels unfinished: it breaks the return phase.

**Best for:** Mythic, transformational, or coming-of-age narratives. Overused in corporate storytelling to the point of parody; use with care outside its natural genre.

## Dan Harmon's Story Circle

Harmon (creator of Community and Rick and Morty) distilled the Hero's Journey into 8 beats, explicitly designed for practical use in series television. It is the most versatile modern compression of the monomyth.

**8 beats (on a circle, not a line):**
1. **You** — establish the protagonist in their zone of comfort
2. **Need** — the protagonist wants or needs something
3. **Go** — they enter an unfamiliar situation to pursue it
4. **Search** — they adapt to the new situation, trying to get what they want
5. **Find** — they get what they wanted
6. **Take** — they pay a price for it
7. **Return** — they return to the familiar situation
8. **Change** — they are now different because of what happened

**The circle matters:** Beats 5–8 mirror beats 1–4 but in reverse. Finding what you wanted (5) costs something (6). The return (7) parallels the departure (3). The change (8) reflects the original want (2) — but the character is transformed.

**Best for:** Any medium where a single episode or unit of content must feel complete. Excellent teaching tool for first drafts.

## Save the Cat (Blake Snyder)

Blake Snyder's *Save the Cat!* (2005) broke Hollywood's 3-act structure into 15 specific beats with page targets. It became the dominant practical screenwriting framework of the 2000s.

**15 beats (page targets for a 110-page screenplay):**
1. Opening image (p. 1) — a visual that captures the before-state
2. Theme stated (p. 5) — someone states the story's thesis, which the protagonist initially ignores
3. Setup (pp. 1–10) — introduce protagonist's world and flaws
4. Catalyst (p. 12) — the inciting incident
5. Debate (pp. 12–25) — protagonist resists the call
6. Break into two (p. 25) — protagonist commits; act 2 begins
7. B story (p. 30) — a secondary story (often the love story) carrying the theme
8. Fun and games (pp. 30–55) — the premise delivers on its promise
9. Midpoint (p. 55) — false victory or false defeat
10. Bad guys close in (pp. 55–75) — obstacles escalate
11. All is lost (p. 75) — the darkest moment; false defeat
12. Dark night of the soul (pp. 75–80) — protagonist's internal low point
13. Break into three (p. 80) — protagonist has a new idea; act 3 begins
14. Finale (pp. 80–110) — protagonist executes the new plan
15. Final image (p. 110) — mirrors the opening; shows how much has changed

**Best for:** Commercial film, TV pilots, narrative marketing, and business presentations that need reliable pacing on a strict runtime.

## Kishōtenketsu

A four-act structure from Chinese poetry (qǐ chéng zhuǎn hé), adapted into Japanese narrative arts (ki-shō-ten-ketsu). Fundamentally different from Western structure in that it does not require central conflict.

**Four acts:**
1. **Ki (Introduction)** — introduce characters and setting; establish the world
2. **Shō (Development)** — develop the introduction; deepen characterization and situation
3. **Ten (Twist)** — introduce a surprising, seemingly unrelated element that recontextualizes everything before it
4. **Ketsu (Reconciliation)** — bring together the elements from acts 1–2 and act 3 to reveal their relationship

**Key difference from Western structures:** The engine is *surprise and resolution*, not *conflict and resolution*. The audience asks not "will the protagonist succeed?" but "how do these apparently unrelated elements connect?"

**Best for:** Short films, literary short stories, essays, product launches (new capability that reframes existing behavior), and any narrative where conflict feels forced or inappropriate.

## StoryBrand (Donald Miller)

Donald Miller's *Building a StoryBrand* (2017) adapted narrative structure specifically for brand and marketing communication. It places the customer — not the brand — as the hero.

**7 parts:**
1. **A character** — the customer who wants something
2. **Has a problem** — external problem (the surface issue), internal problem (how it makes them feel), philosophical problem (why it matters)
3. **Meets a guide** — the brand positions itself as guide (not hero), demonstrating empathy and authority
4. **Who gives them a plan** — a clear, simple process for engaging with the brand
5. **And calls them to action** — a direct, specific call to act
6. **That helps them avoid failure** — what is lost if they don't act
7. **And ends in success** — the transformation they will experience

**Key insight:** Most brands mistakenly position themselves as the hero. Audiences identify with the customer character, not the brand. Brands that position as heroes create confusion; brands that position as guides create trust.

**Best for:** Marketing copy, brand narratives, landing pages, sales presentations, pitch decks to investors or customers.

## Pixar Spine

An internal teaching tool used at Pixar, popularized by Emma Coats in her *22 Rules of Storytelling* (2011). It is the fastest way to test whether a story has a working skeleton.

**The formula:**
> Once upon a time ___. Every day ___. Until one day ___. Because of that ___. Because of that ___. Because of that ___. Until finally ___. And ever since then ___.

Each blank must be filled with something specific and causal. The "because of that" chain is the test: if you cannot generate at least two causal links, your story does not have a working middle.

**Best for:** First-draft story development, testing story ideas, teaching causality, short-form narratives, and as a check before committing to a longer structure.

## Comparison table

| Structure | Domain fit | Conflict required | Complexity | Typical length |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Freytag's Pyramid | Literary fiction, drama | Yes | Low | Novel, play |
| Three-Act | Film, TV, talks, pitches | Yes | Medium | Feature, 30–60 min |
| Fichtean Curve | Thriller, suspense, action | Yes | Medium | Novel, short story, film |
| Hero's Journey | Mythic, fantasy, coming-of-age | Yes | High | Novel, epic film, series |
| Story Circle | TV episode, short-form, any | Yes | Low | Episode, post, short talk |
| Save the Cat | Commercial film, TV pilot | Yes | High | Feature, pilot |
| Kishōtenketsu | Literary, essay, short film | No | Low | Short story, essay, poem |
| StoryBrand | Marketing, brand, sales | Implied | Low | Campaign, landing page |
| Pixar Spine | Any (planning tool) | Yes | Minimal | Diagnostic tool |

## Decision framework

**Use 3-act structure or Save the Cat when:**
- Audience expects Hollywood-style pacing
- You have a strict runtime and need reliable beat placement
- The story is plot-driven with external stakes

**Use Pixar Spine when:**
- You are still developing the idea and need to test its bones
- The story will be short (under 5 minutes)
- You are teaching story structure to others

**Use StoryBrand when:**
- The narrative is for a marketing, sales, or brand purpose
- You need to position a product or company, not a fictional character
- The audience is customers or investors, not a general reading public

**Use Kishōtenketsu when:**
- Central conflict would feel forced or culturally inappropriate
- The story's engine is discovery or recontextualization, not opposition
- The form is short (essay, short story, vignette)

**Use the Hero's Journey or Story Circle when:**
- The story is about personal transformation
- The protagonist must leave their world and return changed
- The narrative has mythic or archetypal dimensions

**Use the Fichtean Curve when:**
- You cannot afford an extended setup (limited attention, competitive context)
- Tension and momentum are the primary experience you want to create
- The genre is thriller, mystery, or suspense

**Use Freytag's Pyramid when:**
- The work is academic, literary, or theater-adjacent
- You want a shared vocabulary with editors, teachers, or literary readers
