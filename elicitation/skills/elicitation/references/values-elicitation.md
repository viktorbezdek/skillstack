# Values Elicitation

> Shalom Schwartz's Theory of Basic Values provides a framework for understanding what actually drives a person — as distinct from what they say drives them. This file covers the 10 universal values and four indirect elicitation techniques that surface them without the performative distortion that direct questioning produces.

---

## Why you cannot just ask people what they value

Direct questions about values ("What are your core values? What's most important to you?") produce answers that reveal what the person thinks values *should* be, not what actually drives their behavior. Everyone claims to value honesty, family, and hard work. The responses are socially desirable, not diagnostic.

Values must be elicited **indirectly** — through stories, contrasts, and decisions where multiple values competed and one won.

---

## Schwartz's 10 Universal Values

Schwartz's cross-cultural research identified ten values that appear across virtually all studied societies, organized into four higher-order dimensions (Openness to Change, Self-Enhancement, Conservation, Self-Transcendence).

| Value | Core concern | Typical expressions |
|---|---|---|
| **Self-Direction** | Independence, freedom of thought and action | Creativity, curiosity, choosing your own path |
| **Stimulation** | Novelty, excitement, challenge | New experiences, variety, thrill |
| **Hedonism** | Pleasure, enjoyment, gratification | Good food, comfort, immediate satisfaction |
| **Achievement** | Success, competence, ambition | Being capable, recognition, demonstrating skill |
| **Power** | Authority, wealth, social status | Influence over others, control of resources, prestige |
| **Security** | Safety, stability, order | Predictability, protection of self and loved ones |
| **Conformity** | Obedience, self-discipline, politeness | Not disturbing social norms, meeting expectations |
| **Tradition** | Respect, commitment, humility | Honoring heritage, religious observance, customs |
| **Benevolence** | Helpfulness, loyalty, forgiveness | Caring for close others, friendship, reliability |
| **Universalism** | Equality, justice, environmental protection | Caring for strangers and the natural world |

**Structural insight:** values on opposite sides of the circle tend to conflict (e.g., Self-Direction vs. Conformity, Benevolence vs. Power). Adjacent values tend to reinforce each other (e.g., Achievement and Power). When analyzing a person's answers, look for which values they repeatedly favor *at the expense of* other values — that trade-off pattern is the signature of their real priorities.

## Four indirect elicitation techniques

### 1. The role model technique
> "Who do you admire? What is it about them specifically?"

**Why it works:** admiration is a projection of values. People admire those who embody what they themselves value (whether or not they live up to it). The specificity in "what is it about them specifically?" prevents the superficial answer and pushes toward the traits that resonate.

**Follow-ups:**
- *"What specifically do they do that you find admirable?"*
- *"Is there something they have that you wish you had more of?"*
- *"If you had to pick one thing about them to be true of yourself, what would it be?"*

**Interpretation:** note which Schwartz values the traits map to. Someone who admires a teacher for *"sticking to her principles even when the school pressured her"* is revealing Self-Direction. Someone who admires an entrepreneur for *"building something from nothing and making it huge"* is revealing Achievement and Power.

### 2. The opposite day technique
> "What kind of person could you never be? What would feel like a betrayal of yourself?"

**Why it works:** values are often more visible at their boundaries than at their centers. Asking what would feel like a betrayal surfaces the values whose violation would be intolerable — which are usually the deepest ones. A person who answers *"I could never be someone who lies to the people who love me"* is revealing Benevolence + Self-Direction more concretely than any direct question about honesty could.

**Follow-ups:**
- *"What would have to happen for you to become that kind of person?"*
- *"Have you ever been close to that? What kept you from going there?"*

### 3. Decision archaeology
> "Think of a hard choice you made. What ultimately tipped the scales?"

**Why it works:** a hard choice is hard because values were in conflict. Whatever tipped the scales is the value that, in that moment, mattered more. The answer is diagnostic because it reveals a *real* trade-off, not a hypothetical one.

**Follow-ups:**
- *"What did you give up to make that choice?"*
- *"What would the other choice have meant?"*
- *"How did it feel afterward?"*

**Interpretation:** someone who took a lower-paying job to be closer to family revealed that Benevolence outweighed Achievement *for them, in that situation*. Someone who turned down a relationship to pursue a career revealed the opposite. Both choices are legitimate — the values diagnosis is what they chose under pressure, not which choice was "right".

### 4. Anger as a values signal
> "What makes you genuinely angry — not annoyed, but morally outraged?"

**Why it works:** moral anger is almost always triggered by a values violation. What you find *unjust* reveals what you hold *just*. Ask people what makes them moderately annoyed and you get petty complaints; ask what outrages them and you get principles.

**Follow-ups:**
- *"When did you last feel that?"*
- *"What is it about that specifically that crosses a line for you?"*
- *"Has there been a time when you had to speak up about it?"*

**Interpretation:** anger at environmental destruction → Universalism. Anger at people being treated unfairly at work → Benevolence + Universalism. Anger at people taking credit for others' work → Achievement + Benevolence. Anger at disrespect toward elders → Tradition + Conformity.

## Combining techniques

A single technique reveals one facet. Use several across a conversation and triangulate:

1. **Start with the role model** (most comfortable, least threatening)
2. **Follow with decision archaeology** (concrete, grounded in real events)
3. **Use opposite day or anger** once rapport is established (these can feel exposing)

Listen for which Schwartz values keep appearing. A person whose role model embodies Self-Direction, whose hardest choice was to leave a stable job to start their own thing, and whose anger is triggered by being told what to do is showing a consistent Self-Direction signature. You can name that pattern back to them and see whether it fits: *"It sounds like independence might be more central to you than almost anything else."*

## What the results are good for

- **Research** — understanding what motivates a population of users
- **Design** — building products whose affordances match what users actually care about
- **Coaching** — helping individuals see patterns in their own choices
- **Writing** — giving characters coherent value systems

**Not good for:**
- **Diagnosis** — values are preferences, not pathologies
- **Prediction of specific behavior** — values bias choices but do not determine them
- **Cross-cultural assumptions** — the structure is universal; the specific content varies
