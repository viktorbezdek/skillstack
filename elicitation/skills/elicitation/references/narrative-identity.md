# Narrative Identity (McAdams)

> Dan McAdams' research shows the *themes* people use in their life stories predict psychological well-being better than the events they describe. This file covers the 8-scene life story interview, narrative themes to listen for, and how to adapt the method for conversational use.

---

## The core finding

Two people can experience the same external event (job loss, divorce, illness) and construct fundamentally different narratives about it. One will tell a **redemption** story ("it was terrible, but it taught me X and led to Y"). The other will tell a **contamination** story ("things were going well until that happened and nothing has been right since"). The *narrative structure* predicts well-being — not the event.

This means asking "what happened to you?" tells you far less than asking "how do you tell the story of what happened to you?" The latter reveals identity; the former just reveals history.

---

## The 8-scene life story interview

McAdams' canonical instrument asks for 8 specific scenes that together form a person's narrative identity:

1. **High Point** — peak experience, most wonderful moment, a scene of joy/excitement/happiness
2. **Low Point** — nadir, most difficult moment, the scene representing the lowest point of the life story
3. **Turning Point** — moment of significant change, where the course of life shifted
4. **Earliest Memory** — the first clear memory the person has
5. **Important Childhood Memory** — vivid memory from before age 12, positive or negative
6. **Important Adolescent Memory** — vivid memory from the teen years
7. **Important Adult Memory** — significant recent memory
8. **One Other Important Memory** — anything else that defines who they are

Each scene is asked with the same structure: *What happened? Who was involved? What were you thinking and feeling? What does this scene say about who you are?*

---

## Why you should not run this as a list

Asking all 8 scenes sequentially produces an interrogation effect. The person becomes conscious of performing "their life story" for you. Instead:

### Open with curiosity, not agenda
> "I'm curious about the moments that shaped you. Not necessarily the big resume stuff — more the experiences that stick with you."

This invites narrative disclosure without prescribing which scenes to produce.

### Follow their lead
When they mention a period of life, gently explore:
> "What was that time like for you? Any particular moments that stand out?"

If they offer a high point, ask for what surrounds it. If they offer a low point, do not immediately ask for a high point "to balance it out" — stay with the low point long enough that the person feels heard.

### Bridge across time
> "That sounds like it mattered. Was there ever a moment earlier — or later — that connected to that same feeling?"

This is the most powerful technique in the toolkit: using a felt emotion as a search key across autobiographical memory. It produces thematically linked scenes rather than chronologically adjacent ones.

### Circle back
If a scene feels undeveloped and the person has moved on, you can return to it later: *"Earlier you mentioned the summer you worked at the farm — I was struck by how you said that. Can I ask you more about that?"* The circle-back signals attention and invites depth they may have held back the first time.

## Narrative themes to listen for

### Agency themes
Evidence of personal power, achievement, mastery, control over one's own life.

Linguistic markers:
- "I decided..."
- "I made it happen..."
- "I pushed through..."
- "I realized I had to take control..."

High agency in a story predicts self-efficacy, achievement orientation, and (in excess) loneliness / difficulty asking for help.

### Communion themes
Evidence of connection, love, belonging, intimacy, being known.

Linguistic markers:
- "We were all together..."
- "I felt so close to..."
- "They really understood me..."
- "It was the whole family..."

High communion predicts relationship satisfaction, group identification, and (in excess) difficulty setting boundaries.

### Redemption sequences
Narrative arc: bad → good. Suffering becomes the source of growth, meaning, or a better future state.

Linguistic markers:
- "It was terrible, but..."
- "Looking back, I'm glad it happened because..."
- "That's what made me who I am..."
- "I wouldn't change it now..."

Redemption themes predict psychological well-being, generativity (caring about future generations), and positive life satisfaction. They are particularly prominent in American autobiographical narrative.

### Contamination sequences
Narrative arc: good → bad. A positive state is spoiled, ruined, or lost — and does not recover.

Linguistic markers:
- "Things were great until..."
- "I thought I was happy, but then..."
- "It ruined everything..."
- "Nothing was ever the same..."

Contamination themes predict depression risk, anxiety, and lower life satisfaction. **Important:** a single contamination memory is not pathological — patterns across multiple scenes carry the signal.

## Interpreting narrative structure

When analyzing a person's narrative identity, look at three layers:

1. **Which themes dominate?** Agency vs. communion, redemption vs. contamination — which arcs appear most often?
2. **Is the narrative coherent?** A coherent identity has scenes that connect to each other and to a larger meaning. A fragmented identity jumps between unrelated scenes with no integrating frame.
3. **What is the narrator's stance toward the story?** Reflective and meaning-making? Defensive? Distanced? The stance is itself data.

## Practical application in non-research contexts

### User research interviews
Use McAdams' scenes as a fallback when a participant is giving surface answers. "Tell me about a time when [relevant product context] really mattered to you" elicits a scene rather than an opinion.

### Conversational AI design
An agent can track which themes a user has offered over time and respond with matching reflections. If a user has consistently offered agency themes, an agent that asks "tell me about a time you needed someone else's help" may feel off-key.

### Personal history products
McAdams' sequence (childhood → adolescence → early adult → current) is a good spine for structured life review. The Haight/Birren stage-specific questions are better for filling out each period. See `life-review-questions.md`.
