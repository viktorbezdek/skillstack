# Life Review Questions

> Barbara Haight's Life Review Interview and James Birren's Guided Autobiography provide structured question sequences for eliciting autobiographical content across life stages. This file covers both methods and how to adapt them for different use contexts.

---

## When to use structured life review

Use this reference when:

- Building a **personal history product** (memoir-support, life story capture, family archive)
- Designing a **longitudinal research interview** that needs to cover a participant's full trajectory
- Running a **therapeutic reminiscence** activity (with proper supervision)
- Creating a **conversational AI** that builds a user model over many sessions
- Writing a **fiction-research interview** where you need to understand a character's whole life

**Do not use** when:
- You only need a single insight (use narrative-identity prompts instead)
- The person has not consented to a long, personal conversation
- Trust is not yet established (stage-based sequencing requires the person to feel safe)

## Haight's Life Review Interview: stage-by-stage

Barbara Haight's structured life review uses the same core questions adapted to each life stage. The sequences below are starting points — adapt them to context and follow the person's energy, not the script.

### Childhood (before 12)

1. What was your home like?
2. What were your parents like?
3. What was your role in the family?
4. What were you like as a child?
5. What did you enjoy doing most?

**Notes on using this stage:**
- Start here only if the person has signaled they want to go back this far. For many people, childhood is tender territory.
- "What was your home like?" is a better opening than "tell me about your childhood" because it invites concrete description rather than abstract summary.
- Listen for whether the person describes people or places first. People-first suggests communion orientation; places-first suggests environmental sensitivity.
- If the person deflects ("nothing interesting happened") — accept the deflection and move on. Coming back to childhood later in the conversation is often more productive than pushing at it.

### Adolescence (12-18)

1. How did your body change? How did you feel about it?
2. What was school like for you?
3. What were your friendships like?
4. What did you dream about becoming?
5. What was hardest about being a teenager?

**Notes on using this stage:**
- Question 1 (body changes) is intimate — only use in trusted contexts, and make clear the person can skip.
- "What did you dream about becoming?" is a powerful prompt because it surfaces the gap between early dreams and current reality. That gap is often where meaning lives.
- The "hardest thing" question is more productive than "worst thing". Hardest invites struggle stories (often with redemption themes); worst invites contamination or trauma that may not be safe to surface.

### Early adulthood (18-30)

1. What was leaving home like?
2. What were your first serious relationships?
3. What work did you do and how did you feel about it?
4. What were your goals during this time?
5. What was the biggest challenge you faced?

**Notes on using this stage:**
- This period covers the **reminiscence bump** (see `self-defining-memories.md`). People typically have the most vivid memories from here. Expect rich material.
- "What was leaving home like?" doubles as an individuation probe — people who had difficult individuation often pause at this question.
- Follow the energy: if the person lights up when describing a particular period, stay there longer. If they rush through a period, they may be skipping something important — or they may genuinely not have much to say about it. Respect both possibilities.

### Middle adulthood (30-60)

1. How did your sense of yourself change?
2. What were your major accomplishments?
3. What losses did you experience?
4. How did your relationships evolve?
5. What did you learn about yourself?

**Notes on using this stage:**
- Questions 1 and 5 (self-knowledge questions) are particularly productive in this stage — mid-life is when people typically have done enough living to have reflective distance.
- The loss question is necessary because this period usually includes the death of parents and sometimes partners or peers. Hold space for grief if it arrives.
- "How did your relationships evolve?" is gentler than asking about specific relationships — it lets the person choose which ones to talk about.

### Later life (60+)

1. How has your daily life changed?
2. What matters most to you now?
3. What legacy do you want to leave?
4. What do you understand now that you didn't before?
5. What would you tell your younger self?

**Notes on using this stage:**
- These questions can generate the most meaning-laden material of any stage. People in later life have had time to integrate experience and often have answers they have been waiting to share.
- "What matters most to you now?" is a direct values question that works in this stage when it would not work earlier — because older adults have typically done the work of figuring out the answer.
- Question 5 ("what would you tell your younger self?") is a clean ending because it invites the person to formulate wisdom without pretending to be an oracle.

## Birren's Guided Autobiography: themes

James Birren's approach organizes questions by **theme** rather than by life stage. Use this when you want to trace a single concern across a whole life, or when the person finds chronological structure restrictive.

### Family theme
- What was the emotional climate of your home?
- Who were you closest to? Who did you clash with?
- What family stories get told and retold?

### Work theme
- What does work mean to you beyond earning money?
- When have you felt most fulfilled professionally?
- What work would you do even if you weren't paid?

### Money theme
- What were the messages about money in your family?
- What does financial security mean to you?
- What would you do if money were no object?

### Health theme
- How has your relationship with your body changed?
- What health experiences shaped how you think about life?
- How do you take care of yourself?

### Death theme
- Have you experienced significant losses?
- How do thoughts of mortality affect how you live?
- What do you want to be remembered for?

**Caution:** the death theme is the most sensitive. Only use when:
- The person has signaled they are willing to go there
- You have time to stay with whatever comes up
- You are not about to end the conversation immediately after (do not open grief and then leave)

### Meaning theme
- What gives your life meaning?
- What beliefs or values guide you?
- What questions are you still trying to answer?

## Combining methods

In practice, the two methods work well together. A typical structure:

1. **Open with a Birren theme question** — less intrusive, lets the person choose what to share first
2. **When a story surfaces, follow it chronologically** using Haight stage questions if the person wants to go deeper into the period
3. **Bridge across stages thematically** when the person mentions a pattern — *"You talked about feeling invisible at your first job. Earlier you mentioned feeling invisible in high school. Is there a thread there for you?"*

This combination gives you both horizontal coverage (themes) and vertical depth (stages).

---

## Pacing and session length

Life review questions can be exhausting. A single session should generally not exceed **60-90 minutes**, and the first session should be shorter (45 minutes is safer) to let the person calibrate how much depth they want to share.

If you are running a multi-session engagement, a typical sequence is:
- **Session 1:** themes, surface-level — establish safety and rapport
- **Session 2:** chronological, early life — childhood and adolescence
- **Session 3:** chronological, middle life — early adult through present
- **Session 4:** integration — what patterns did we see? what mattered most?

This is the standard structured life review arc developed by Haight. It is not prescriptive — adapt to context.
