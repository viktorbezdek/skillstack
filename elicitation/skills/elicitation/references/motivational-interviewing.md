# Motivational Interviewing: OARS Framework

> Miller & Rollnick developed Motivational Interviewing for clinical behavior change, but its core discovery applies to any depth conversation: reflections outperform questions at eliciting authentic self-disclosure. This file covers the OARS framework and the 2:1 reflection-to-question ratio that should govern any elicitation conversation.

---

## The core discovery

Counselors and researchers who ask lots of questions get factual answers. Counselors and researchers who *reflect* what they hear get elaboration, nuance, and eventually the thing the person was not initially willing to say. This is not a trick — it is a structural property of conversation.

Questions feel like tests. Reflections feel like being understood. People disclose to people who understand them.

---

## OARS — the four core skills

### O: Open questions
Questions that cannot be answered with yes/no. **Use sparingly.** Every question you ask is one the person has to answer instead of being understood.

Instead of: *"Did you like your childhood?"*
Try: *"What was it like growing up in your family?"*

Even better — lead with a reflection of something already said, then invite elaboration without a formal question:
*"You mentioned your sister earlier. That felt like someone who mattered."* (Pause. Let the silence invite.)

### A: Affirmations
Genuine recognition of strengths, efforts, or values. **Not compliments.** A compliment evaluates the person; an affirmation names a trait or action they have already demonstrated.

Instead of: *"That's great!"*
Try: *"You valued honesty even when it was costly."*

Affirmations work because they show you were listening for something meaningful rather than tracking the conversation superficially. Empty praise signals the opposite.

### R: Reflections — this is the core skill
Restate or reframe what the person said. The whole MI method turns on getting reflections right. There are four types, in increasing order of depth:

#### Simple reflection (repeat back)
> Person: "I just felt invisible in that moment."
> You: "Invisible."

or slightly fuller:
> You: "So you felt invisible in that moment."

The simple reflection is almost a verbal nod. It signals "I heard you" without adding anything. Use it early in a conversation when trust is still forming.

#### Complex reflection (add meaning)
Adds an interpretation the person did not explicitly state but that follows from what they said.

> Person: "I just felt invisible in that moment."
> You: "It sounds like recognition really matters to you — like you need to know your contributions are seen."

This invites the person to either confirm or correct the interpretation. Both answers are useful. Correction is especially useful — it sharpens your understanding and shows you are genuinely listening rather than projecting.

#### Amplified reflection (gently exaggerate)
State the person's position slightly more strongly than they did. This is the most delicate move in the toolkit — it invites them to nuance their own position.

> Person: "I don't think my parents really supported my choices."
> You: "So nothing they could have done would have made a difference."

If the reflection is amplified correctly, the person will often walk it back: *"Well, no, not nothing. They did try in some ways — they just didn't understand what I actually needed."* That walk-back is the more nuanced truth.

**Warning:** amplified reflections must be delivered without sarcasm and without judgment. If the person feels mocked, they will shut down. Use only when rapport is strong.

#### Double-sided reflection (hold both truths)
Acknowledge the tension between two things the person has said.

> You: "On one hand, you loved the stability. On the other, you felt trapped by it."

This works because ambivalence is the norm — people rarely feel only one thing. Naming both sides creates space for them to explore the tension without having to pretend the contradictory feeling does not exist.

### S: Summaries
Periodically gather together what you have heard. Summaries create meaning, invite correction, and signal that you are paying attention across time rather than just moment-to-moment.

> "Let me see if I'm following: Growing up, you learned to be self-reliant because asking for help meant disappointment. But you've also noticed that pattern keeping people at a distance now. And you're wondering if there's another way."

A good summary does three things:
1. **Recaps the factual content** ("learned to be self-reliant")
2. **Names the emotional thread** ("asking for help meant disappointment")
3. **Points to what is unresolved** ("wondering if there's another way")

End a summary with a light invitation: *"Does that sound right?"* or just a pause. Let the person correct, add, or redirect.

## The 2:1 ratio

**Aim for 2 reflections for every question.**

This is the operational target that translates the MI philosophy into concrete practice. Most interviewers invert it — they ask 3-4 questions for every reflection — which is why most interviews feel like interrogations.

### Bad pattern (all questions)
> Q: "What happened?"
> Q: "How did that feel?"
> Q: "What did you do next?"

The person feels processed. They answer tersely. Nothing deepens.

### Better pattern (2:1)
> Q: "What happened?"
> R: "That caught you off guard."
> R: "You weren't sure what to make of it."

After the two reflections, the person will almost always continue talking without needing another question. The silence after a good reflection is an invitation, not an awkward gap.

---

## When you do need to ask questions

Not all questions are equal. In descending order of usefulness:

1. **Reflective question** — a reflection with a question mark at the end: *"It sounds like that really mattered to you?"*
2. **Invitation** — *"Is there more to that?"* or *"What else comes to mind?"*
3. **Bridging question** — connects to something earlier: *"You mentioned the summer at the farm earlier — was this connected to that?"*
4. **Exploratory question** — open and broad: *"What was that time like for you?"*
5. **Targeted question** — specific and concrete: *"How old were you?"*
6. **Closed question** — yes/no: *"Did that upset you?"*

Closed questions have their place (confirming a fact, closing a topic) but should be rare.

---

## Reflections are not parroting

A common mistake is to reflect the same words back verbatim. That feels mechanical and breaks the conversational frame. Instead:

- **Change the emphasis** to reveal what mattered: *Person: "I worked really hard on that project."* → *You: "It really meant something to you."*
- **Translate to feeling**: *Person: "My parents always wanted me to become a doctor."* → *You: "That expectation was always in the room."*
- **Reflect the emotional texture rather than the facts**: *Person: "We moved every two years growing up."* → *You: "So you never really had a place to call home."*

A good reflection names something the person felt but did not say. A perfect reflection names something the person felt but did not *know* they felt.

---

## Resistance is information

If the person pushes back on a reflection — "no, that's not quite it" — this is not a failure. It is the most useful possible outcome. They now have to articulate the more precise version of what they meant, which is usually closer to the truth than what they originally said.

Never defend a reflection. Never argue. If they correct you, say *"thank you, that makes it clearer"* and use their correction as the new starting point.
