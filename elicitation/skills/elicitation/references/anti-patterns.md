# Elicitation Anti-Patterns

> Every anti-pattern in this file is common, comes from good intentions, and destroys the conditions for authentic self-disclosure. Use this file to audit your own draft conversations and critique others' — always naming the specific anti-pattern and showing the concrete fix, not just "avoid this".

---

## How to use this reference

When reviewing a conversation script, transcript, or AI prompt, scan for each anti-pattern below. When you find one:

1. **Name it specifically** — "This is the Interrogation Trap, not just 'too many questions'"
2. **Point to the concrete line** that exhibits it
3. **Show the fix** — not "do less of this" but "replace this line with this line"
4. **Explain why it matters** — the mechanism by which the anti-pattern degrades disclosure

Vague critiques produce vague improvements. Specific critiques produce actionable revisions.

---

## 1. The Interrogation Trap

**Pattern:** rapid-fire questions with little or no reflection between them. The conversation feels like a job interview or police questioning.

**Why it happens:** interviewers feel time pressure. They have a list of questions to get through. Reflection feels "slow" and like it's not "making progress".

**Why it destroys disclosure:** questions feel extractive. People become guarded, answer briefly, and wait for the next question. The conversation stays on the surface because the interviewer is signaling "I want information" rather than "I want to understand".

**What it looks like:**
> Q: "What happened?"
> Q: "How did that feel?"
> Q: "What did you do next?"
> Q: "Did anyone else know?"
> Q: "What would you do differently?"

**The fix:** target a 2:1 reflection-to-question ratio. See `motivational-interviewing.md`. For every question you ask, follow the answer with one or two reflections that show you understood before moving on.

> Q: "What happened?"
> R: "That caught you off guard."
> R: "You weren't sure what to make of it."
> (Pause. Let the silence invite elaboration.)

**Self-audit question:** count the questions and reflections in your script. If questions outnumber reflections, you have this problem.

## 2. The Interpretation Leap

**Pattern:** jumping to psychological conclusions before you have evidence — naming a schema, labeling a personality trait, or diagnosing a pattern based on a single statement.

**Why it happens:** pattern-recognition is rewarding. Once you learn about schemas, everything looks schema-shaped. Interviewers who have just read about Schwartz's values start seeing them everywhere.

**Why it destroys disclosure:** naming someone's pattern back to them — especially a critical pattern — feels like being caught or labeled. People become defensive, either pushing back or retreating into performance.

**What it looks like:**
> Person: "My parents were always pretty hands-off."
> You: "It sounds like you developed a strong attachment avoidance style early on."

**The fix:** hold hypotheses lightly. When you notice a pattern, treat it as a *tentative read* that needs confirming evidence from multiple moments in the conversation. And when you do reflect the pattern, do so neutrally and invitationally:

> Person: "My parents were always pretty hands-off."
> You: "Hands-off. That sounds like it shaped what you expected from people."

Now the person can confirm, correct, or redirect. You have not labeled them; you have offered a direction they can accept or decline.

**Self-audit question:** am I naming something about the person, or reflecting what they said about themselves? The latter is safe; the former is an interpretation leap.

## 3. The Agenda Push

**Pattern:** steering the conversation toward topics you think are important, rather than following the person's energy and emphasis.

**Why it happens:** interviewers come in with a plan. The plan has priorities. When the person wanders off the priority topics, the instinct is to bring them back.

**Why it destroys disclosure:** the person's emphasis *is* the data. What they naturally return to, what they spend more time on, what they mention without being asked — all of this reveals what matters to them. When you steer away from their emphasis, you are losing the signal you came for.

**What it looks like:**
> You (planned topic: career): "Tell me about your work history."
> Person: "Well, I was at Google for a while, but honestly the most important thing was when my mother got sick and I had to move back home for two years."
> You: "Right, but in terms of your career, what was the progression at Google?"

**The fix:** let them lead. Their shift to the mother's illness is the data. Follow it. You can return to the planned topics later if they still matter — and often you will find the planned topics are answered by what surfaces when you follow the energy.

> You: "Two years is a long time. What was that period like for you?"

**Self-audit question:** when the person steers off-script, is my instinct to follow or redirect? If redirect, I have this problem.

## 4. Premature Depth

**Pattern:** asking deeply personal or emotionally charged questions before trust has been established.

**Why it happens:** interviewers want to "get to the good stuff". They worry about running out of time. They feel awkward with small talk and want to move quickly to substance.

**Why it destroys disclosure:** trust is a prerequisite for depth. Asking a deep question of a stranger produces either a performative answer (what they think you want to hear) or a shut-down response (refusal or deflection). Neither is useful. Worse, the stranger now associates you with a feeling of being intruded upon — and they will calibrate all future answers accordingly.

**What it looks like:**
> (Five minutes into a first conversation)
> You: "So tell me about your biggest regret in life."

**The fix:** earn disclosure gradually. Start with neutral territory (context, current situation, recent events). Move to mid-depth territory (preferences, values, low-stakes opinions). Only go to high-depth territory (formative memories, schemas, regrets, losses) once the person has signaled they are ready.

Signals that depth is welcome:
- The person has already offered a moderately personal disclosure without being asked
- The person asks a personal question of you (mutual disclosure is a signal)
- The person pauses meaningfully on a topic and does not change the subject
- The person's body language (if visible) or voice (if audible) has softened

**Self-audit question:** would I feel comfortable if someone I just met asked me this question in this order? If not, it is too soon.

## 5. Therapy Cosplay

**Pattern:** using clinical language or techniques that imply you are treating the person — schema labels, diagnostic terminology, therapeutic framings like "processing" or "unpacking".

**Why it happens:** interviewers who have studied therapeutic methods often adopt therapeutic language without realizing it creates a clinical frame. The person now feels like a patient rather than a collaborator.

**Why it destroys disclosure:** people disclose differently to therapists than to researchers or friends. Therapy cosplay creates an artificial dynamic where the person expects you to "help" them — which you cannot do (you are not their therapist) — and adjusts their disclosure accordingly, often producing therapy-shaped answers.

**What it looks like:**
> You: "I wonder if we can unpack that feeling a bit. Where in your body do you notice it?"
> You: "It sounds like you're processing some deep attachment wounds."

**The fix:** be curious, not clinical. You are learning about them, not diagnosing or treating them. Plain language is almost always better than therapeutic jargon.

> You: "That sounds like a lot to carry. Can you tell me more about what it felt like at the time?"

**Self-audit question:** am I using words I only learned from reading psychology? If so, would a plain-language version work just as well? (Usually yes.)

## 6. The Monologue Response

**Pattern:** responding to someone's disclosure by launching into your own lengthy story on a similar theme.

**Why it happens:** sharing a matching story feels like empathy. "Me too!" feels like connection. And if the matching story is good, it can be hard to resist telling it.

**Why it destroys disclosure:** the conversation is about them, not you. When you respond to their vulnerable disclosure with a long story of your own, you have taken the focus away from them. Next time they start to open up, they will remember that opening up means listening to your story — and they will disclose less.

**What it looks like:**
> Person: "I felt like I'd let everyone down when I quit that job."
> You: "Oh, I completely understand. When I quit my first tech job, I felt the same way — my parents had worked so hard to pay for my education, and here I was walking away. I remember telling them on the phone, and my father's silence felt like a judgment. It took me years to..."

**The fix:** keep the focus on them. Brief self-disclosure can build rapport (one or two sentences, strategically), but always return to them immediately.

> Person: "I felt like I'd let everyone down when I quit that job."
> You: "Letting people down. That's a heavy thing to carry."
> (Pause.)
> Person: [continues elaborating, because you left space for them]

**Self-audit question:** when the person discloses, does my next turn spend more time on me or on them? If on me, I have this problem.

## 7. The Validation Trap

**Pattern:** agreeing with everything the person says, offering constant encouragement, never gently challenging.

**Why it happens:** interviewers worry about maintaining rapport. They fear that any disagreement or nuance will break the connection. So they validate reflexively.

**Why it destroys disclosure:** constant validation feels sycophantic. The person stops trusting your reflections because they know you would have agreed regardless. Worse, the person misses the opportunity to nuance their own position — which is where meaning-making happens.

**What it looks like:**
> Person: "I think I'm just a really generous person."
> You: "Absolutely, that comes through so clearly. It's wonderful."

**The fix:** genuine reflections can gently challenge without confrontation. The amplified reflection (see `motivational-interviewing.md`) is particularly useful here.

> Person: "I think I'm just a really generous person."
> You: "So generosity comes naturally — you never feel it costs you anything?"

Now the person has space to either confirm (*"Well, no — sometimes I resent it, actually..."*) or nuance (*"I mean, I do have to push myself sometimes..."*). Both answers are more revealing than "absolutely, that's wonderful".

**Self-audit question:** in the last five reflections I wrote, did any of them gently push back or invite nuance? If all five were pure validation, I have this problem.

## Meta-anti-pattern: applying this file mechanically

A final anti-pattern: treating this file as a checklist to apply rigidly. These patterns overlap, interact, and sometimes conflict. A conversation that tries to eliminate all seven simultaneously becomes stilted and mechanical — which is its own anti-pattern.

Use this file as a diagnostic lens, not a ruleset. When a conversation feels flat, shallow, or extractive, scan through the seven and ask *which one is most present here?* Fix the most present one first. Then re-evaluate.

## Quick audit table

When reviewing a conversation or script, run through this table:

| Anti-pattern | Signal to watch for |
|---|---|
| Interrogation Trap | Questions-to-reflections ratio > 1:1 |
| Interpretation Leap | Any sentence that names a psychological trait about the person |
| Agenda Push | Any move that redirects from what the person just said |
| Premature Depth | A deep question before any trust-building exchange |
| Therapy Cosplay | Clinical vocabulary (schema, processing, unpacking, attachment style) |
| Monologue Response | Interviewer's turns longer than the person's turns |
| Validation Trap | Reflections that are all affirming, none gently challenging |

Score any conversation on all seven. The conversation that scores clean on all seven is the one worth designing toward.
