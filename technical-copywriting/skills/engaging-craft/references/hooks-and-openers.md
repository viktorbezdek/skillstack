# Hooks and Openers

A hook is the first 50–200 words. Its job: get the reader to the next paragraph. Most articles fail not because of bad bodies but because of dead openings — and most openings die from the same handful of patterns. The fix is to pick a hook *type* deliberately rather than starting with throat-clearing.

## The eight hook types

### 1. The misconception

State a widely-held view; signal that it's wrong or incomplete.

```
"Most engineers will tell you that microservices give you independent deployments. That's true.
 But it conceals a more important effect — and it's not the one you'd guess."
```

**Works for:** deep-dives, opinion.
**Avoid for:** tutorials (the tutorial reader doesn't want to be told their model is wrong; they want to do the thing).

**Why it works:** the reader brings their own version of the misconception; you're playing on their existing mental model. Curiosity is generated by the gap between what they think and what you're about to show.

**Failure mode:** if the "misconception" isn't actually widely held, the hook lands as a strawman. Test: would three peers agree this is a common belief?

### 2. The surprising fact

Specific, verifiable, contradicts the reader's prior.

```
"In 2023, the average Postgres database in production had 18% dead tuples. Not 2%. Not 5%.
 Eighteen percent. Here's what's eating the rest of your disk."
```

**Works for:** deep-dives, case studies, opinion.
**Avoid for:** narratives (breaks the in-medias-res frame).

**Why it works:** specificity. "Many databases have problems" doesn't grip; "18%" does. Citable specificity also signals research depth.

**Failure mode:** the fact must be cited and defensible. A surprising-but-wrong opening damages credibility for the whole piece.

### 3. The in-medias-res

Open in the middle of an event, with sensory specifics.

```
"At 02:47 AM on a Sunday, our database stopped accepting writes. The error message wasn't in
 our runbook. The on-call engineer was three minutes from her child's school recital."
```

**Works for:** technical narratives, case studies, postmortems.
**Avoid for:** explainers, tutorials, whitepapers.

**Why it works:** the scene puts the reader in a moment. The specific details (02:47, runbook, school recital) ground the story.

**Failure mode:** generic "Imagine you're an on-call engineer" openings are not in-medias-res; they're roleplay prompts. The hook needs *real* specifics, not invited imagination.

### 4. The question

Ask something the reader has implicitly wondered.

```
"You know that long-running transactions are bad for Postgres. Have you ever looked at
 exactly *why* — at the level of which file gets dirty?"
```

**Works for:** deep-dives, tutorials.
**Avoid for:** opinion (questions weaken assertion-mode pieces).

**Why it works:** the question converts diffuse curiosity ("I should learn more about this") into focused interest ("here's a specific thing I want to know").

**Failure mode:** rhetorical questions the reader didn't actually wonder. "Have you ever wondered how the internet works?" is presumptuous; nobody asked.

### 5. The artifact

Open with code, output, a chart, a screenshot.

```
SELECT pg_size_pretty(pg_database_size('mydb'));
 pg_size_pretty
----------------
 1247 GB
(1 row)

"This database has 200 GB of data. The other 1,000 GB is something else."
```

**Works for:** deep-dives, tutorials, case studies.
**Avoid for:** narrative-driven pieces (the artifact breaks the narrative frame).

**Why it works:** visual. Bypasses text fatigue. Creates curiosity through the gap (200 vs 1,247).

**Failure mode:** the artifact must be self-explanatory. A complex chart that requires three paragraphs of setup before the reader understands what they're looking at fails as a hook.

### 6. The failed expectation

Show what someone tried, what they expected, what actually happened.

```
"We added an index. We measured the queries. The slow ones got faster. The total system
 throughput got *worse*. That's the story."
```

**Works for:** case studies, technical narratives, opinion.
**Avoid for:** tutorials (the failure-mode opening sets the wrong expectation for a how-to).

**Why it works:** narrative arc compressed into three sentences. The reader wants to know how A→B→C happened.

**Failure mode:** the expectation reversal must be honest. Manufactured "surprise" results read as clickbait.

### 7. The consequential question

Open with a question that has stakes for the reader.

```
"If your CI takes 12 minutes, what does that cost your team per quarter? The honest answer is
 more than you'd guess — and it compounds."
```

**Works for:** opinion, decision-support pieces.
**Avoid for:** deep-dives (consequences are usually too vague to anchor an explainer).

**Why it works:** stakes. "How does this work?" is intellectual; "what does this cost you?" is personal.

**Failure mode:** if the answer turns out to be "actually not much," the article disappoints. Use only when the costs are real and citable.

### 8. The intellectual provocation

A claim that's surprising or contrarian; the article defends it.

```
"There is no such thing as a microservices architecture. There is only the cost of distributed
 systems being paid by every team that adopts them — and most teams don't realize they're
 paying it."
```

**Works for:** opinion.
**Avoid for:** anything else.

**Why it works:** intellectual conflict. The reader either nods or argues; both engage them.

**Failure mode:** the claim must be defended honestly. A provocation that turns out to be a hot take with no evidence is the worst possible opening — it spent attention and didn't deliver.

## Hook selection

Match the hook to the template:

| Template | Best hook types | Avoid |
|---|---|---|
| Deep-dive | Misconception, surprising fact, artifact, question | In-medias-res, intellectual provocation |
| Tutorial | Artifact, failed expectation, problem statement | Intellectual provocation, opinion-mode hooks |
| Opinion | Misconception, intellectual provocation, consequential question | Tutorial-mode openers |
| Case study | In-medias-res, surprising fact, failed expectation | Intellectual provocation |
| Whitepaper | Surprising fact, executive summary, framing question | In-medias-res, intellectual provocation |
| Technical narrative | In-medias-res, failed expectation | Surprising-fact (kills the narrative) |

## Hook anti-patterns

### "In today's fast-paced digital landscape…"

Generic-prose tell. Reader leaves before the third sentence.

### "Have you ever wondered…"

Forced rhetorical question. Most readers haven't wondered the thing.

### "We all know that…"

Inclusive presumption. Often what follows isn't actually known.

### "It's no secret that…"

Throat-clearing. If it's not a secret, just state the fact.

### "Picture this:" / "Imagine you're a…"

Roleplay prompts. The reader didn't sign up to imagine. Real specifics beat invited imagination.

### "Software is eating the world."

Quote-as-hook. Famous quotes have been used too often; the reader recognizes the move and discounts.

### The autobiographical preamble

```
"I've been writing software for 15 years. I started in PHP, then Ruby, then Rust. Along the
 way, I've learned a few things about…"
```

Establish credibility through the substance of the piece, not its preface.

### The throat-clearing definition

```
"Before we dive in, let's define what we mean by 'caching.' Caching is the process of storing
 frequently-accessed data in a location that allows for faster retrieval…"
```

The reader didn't come for definitions. Define inline as you need terms.

### The teaser without payoff

```
"I'm going to show you something that will change how you think about Postgres forever."
```

Promise inflation. Specific concrete promises beat hyperbolic vague ones.

### The genre-mismatch hook

A tutorial that opens with an opinion-style provocation. A whitepaper that opens with in-medias-res. The reader's expectations get scrambled before they've started.

## Hook engineering process

### Step 1: pick the hook type

Based on template and audience awareness level (see `proven-formulas.md` for Schwartz). Don't start drafting until you know which type.

### Step 2: draft 3 candidate hooks

Each should be 50–150 words. Write them quickly; perfection comes later. Variations to try:

- Vary length: one tight (50 words), one medium (100), one expansive (150).
- Vary specificity: one concrete-led, one question-led, one image-led.
- Vary tone: one urgent, one detached, one personal.

### Step 3: read each aloud

The hook that reads cleanest aloud usually wins. Halting prose at the start of an article is a bad sign — the reader feels the halt.

### Step 4: test against the promise

Does the hook lead naturally into the promise? If there's a gap between the hook's energy and the promise's content, fix one of them. Often the right fix is to rewrite the promise, not the hook.

### Step 5: commit

The hook is now load-bearing. Don't keep tinkering during drafting; let the rest of the piece flow from the committed hook. Revisit only in revision.

## Worked openers, by hook type

### Misconception (deep-dive)

```
"The conventional wisdom about strict mode in TypeScript is that the migration cost dominates
 the type-safety benefit for most teams. That's true — for the first two weeks. Past two weeks,
 the curves cross."
```

### Surprising fact (case study)

```
"We migrated 100,234 tests from Mocha to Vitest in 11 weeks. Three of them broke production.
 Not three thousand — three. This is the story of how three is so few that it shouldn't have
 been possible."
```

### In-medias-res (narrative)

```
"At 02:47 AM on a Sunday, our database stopped accepting writes. The on-call engineer was
 three minutes from her child's school recital. The error message wasn't in the runbook."
```

### Question (deep-dive)

```
"You know that VACUUM is important. What you may not know is what determines *when* it can run
 — and the answer ties directly to that idle psql window you've had open since lunch."
```

### Artifact (deep-dive)

```
$ kubectl get pods -A | wc -l
 247

"In a healthy 247-pod cluster, the most common reason for the 248th pod to fail to schedule is
 not what you'd think. It's not resources. It's not affinity. Here's what it usually is."
```

### Failed expectation (case study)

```
"We added 12 indexes to fix slow queries. Eleven of them helped. The twelfth made the entire
 database 30% slower. Here's what we got wrong about index selectivity."
```

### Consequential question (opinion)

```
"What if the most expensive engineering decision your team made this year was choosing
 microservices three years ago — and you're paying down the bill in coordination cost without
 realizing it?"
```

### Intellectual provocation (opinion)

```
"There is no such thing as a 100%-test-coverage codebase that's actually 100% covered. The
 number lies. Here's how, and what to measure instead."
```

## Hooks for sequels and series

When writing part 2 of a series, the hook does extra work — it has to re-anchor for new readers and re-energize returning readers.

### Re-anchor for new readers

Open with one paragraph that summarizes what the reader needs from part 1, plus a link.

```
"In part 1, we showed that long-running transactions block VACUUM, which lets dead tuples
 accumulate, which fills your disk. (If you missed it: [link]). In part 2: the production
 alerting that catches this before it costs you a disk."
```

### Re-energize returning readers

Open with a payoff or surprise that the part-1 reader's been waiting for.

```
"At the end of part 1, we left a question hanging: what would happen if you let the dead tuples
 accumulate for a month? We let them. Here's what we found."
```

## Hooks that earn their position

A great hook does two things at once:

1. **Creates a question** that the article answers.
2. **Establishes the article's voice** in microcosm.

The reader, after the hook, should know: what question is being asked, and roughly how the writer is going to handle it. If the hook does only one of these, it's working at half capacity.

## The hook revision pass

After the article is drafted, revisit the hook. Often the hook you started with reflects an early version of the piece; the actual piece you wrote may want a different opening.

Specifically:

- **Did the article shift its angle during writing?** Update the hook to match.
- **Did the payoff turn out to be different than planned?** The hook needs to set up the *actual* payoff.
- **Did the voice shift?** Make hook and body match.
- **Is there a better hook *inside* the article you could promote?** A specific scene from section 3, a striking number from section 5, can sometimes become the new opening.

The hook is the most-rewritten part of any well-engineered article. Don't fall in love with the first draft.
