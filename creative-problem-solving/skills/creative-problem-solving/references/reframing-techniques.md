# Reframing Techniques

## Meta-Level Reframes

### Chunk Up (Abstract)
Generalize to broader category/purpose.
- "What is this an example of?"
- "What's the purpose of this?"
- "At a higher level, what are we really doing?"

### Chunk Down (Specific)
Break into components or examples.
- "What are specific instances?"
- "What are the parts of this?"
- "Can you give me an example?"

### Chunk Sideways (Analogies)
Find parallel examples at same level.
- "What else is like this?"
- "Where have we solved similar problems?"
- "What's the X of Y?" (Uber of healthcare, etc.)

## Perspective Shifts

### Stakeholder Rotation
View from each affected party's position.
- Customers, employees, competitors, suppliers, regulators
- "What would [stakeholder] want/fear?"
- Identify conflicting incentives

### Time Horizons
Examine at different time scales.
- **Immediate**: What happens today/this week?
- **Near-term**: 1-6 months
- **Mid-term**: 1-3 years  
- **Long-term**: 5+ years
- **Generational**: What legacy does this create?

### Scale Shifts
Change magnitude dramatically.
- "What if we had 10x more/less?"
- "What if this had to serve 1 person? 1 million?"
- "What if budget was 1% or 100x current?"

### Reverse Thinking
Invert the problem.
- "How would we guarantee failure?"
- "What would make this worse?"
- Then avoid/do opposite

### Best/Worst Case
Explore extremes to find range.
- "What's the best possible outcome?"
- "What's the worst that could happen?"
- "What's the most likely outcome?"
- Use to calibrate risk/reward

## Context Reframes

### Different Domains
Apply thinking from other fields.
- Military strategy → Business competition
- Biology → Organizations (ecosystems, evolution)
- Physics → Social dynamics
- Engineering → Problem-solving processes

### Cultural Lenses
How would different cultures approach this?
- Eastern vs. Western thinking
- Collectivist vs. Individualist
- High/Low context communication
- Risk tolerance variations

### Historical Parallels
Has this happened before?
- "When has something similar occurred?"
- "What did we learn last time?"
- "What patterns repeat?"
- Beware: History rhymes, doesn't repeat exactly

## Problem Type Reframes

### From Problem to Opportunity
Flip negative to positive.
- "What advantage does this difficulty create?"
- "Who would pay to have this problem?"
- "What can we do now that we couldn't before?"

### From Feature to Bug (and vice versa)
What seems bad/good from different angles?
- "When is this 'limitation' actually beneficial?"
- "When does this 'feature' cause problems?"
- Context-dependent value

### From Technical to Human
Shift from mechanism to meaning.
- Not "improve algorithm" but "help users find what they need"
- Not "reduce latency" but "save people's time"
- Connect technical work to human impact

### From Individual to Systemic
Personal problem → System property.
- "Is this a person problem or a system problem?"
- "If many face this, what structure causes it?"
- "Can we change the system vs. training individuals?"

## Constraint Reframes

### Fixed to Variable
What if "unchangeable" could change?
- "What would it take to modify this constraint?"
- "Is this truly fixed or just assumed fixed?"
- Political, physical, or conventional constraints?

### Addition to Subtraction
Instead of "what to add," ask "what to remove."
- "What can we stop doing?"
- "What's unnecessary complexity?"
- Via negativa: Remove harm before adding benefit

### OR to AND
False dichotomies.
- "Can we have both?"
- "What's the third option we haven't considered?"
- "How do we transcend this trade-off?"

## Question Reframes

### Problem to Question
Convert statements to questions.
- "We need more revenue" → "How might we increase revenue?"
- Opens possibilities vs. fixed framing

### Better Questions
Upgrade question quality.
- Add constraints: "Without spending money..."
- Remove constraints: "If we had unlimited resources..."
- Change success metric: "Instead of measuring X, what if we measure Y?"
- Multiple angles: "What's the real problem? What problem does that problem cause?"

### Five Whys
Drill to root cause.
- Ask "Why?" five times
- Each answer becomes next question
- Stop when you hit fundamental cause
- Beware: May have multiple root causes
