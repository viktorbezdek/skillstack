# Strategic Analysis Frameworks

## Game Theory Analysis

### Nash Equilibrium
Identify stable states where no player benefits from unilateral change.
- Map each party's incentives and payoffs
- Find where optimal strategies intersect
- Consider: "If everyone acts optimally, what happens?"

### Prisoner's Dilemma Patterns
Recognize cooperation vs. defection dynamics.
- Individual rational → Collective suboptimal
- Solutions: Repeat games, reputation, contracts, changing payoffs
- Ask: "How can we align individual and collective incentives?"

### Zero-Sum vs. Positive-Sum
Determine if competition is necessary or value can be created.
- Zero-sum: Fixed pie, pure competition
- Positive-sum: Expandable pie, cooperation opportunities
- Reframe: "Can we change the game from zero-sum to positive-sum?"

### Backwards Induction
Solve from end-state backwards to find optimal path.
- Start with desired outcome
- Work backwards: "What must happen before this?"
- Identify critical decision points

### Strategic Commitment
Use irreversible actions to gain advantage.
- Burning bridges to force focus
- Public commitments to build credibility
- First-mover advantages vs. costs

## First Principles Thinking

### Deconstruction
Break problem to fundamental truths.
1. Identify and challenge all assumptions
2. Break down to basic facts that cannot be reduced
3. Reason up from foundations

Example: "What are we really trying to accomplish?" not "How is this usually done?"

### Physics of the Problem
What are the fundamental constraints?
- Laws of nature (thermodynamics, information theory)
- Mathematical limits (computational complexity)
- Human factors (cognition, psychology)

### Cost Buildup
For pricing/feasibility, reconstruct from base components.
- What are raw materials/inputs?
- What transformations are required?
- What's the theoretical minimum cost/effort?

## Systems Thinking

### Feedback Loops
Identify reinforcing and balancing loops.
- **Reinforcing**: Growth or decline accelerates (virtuous/vicious cycles)
- **Balancing**: Self-correcting toward equilibrium
- Look for delays: Effects lag causes

### Leverage Points
Find where small changes create large effects (Meadows hierarchy).
- **Strongest**: Paradigms, goals, self-organization
- **Medium**: Information flows, rules, feedback strength
- **Weakest**: Parameters, buffers, stocks

### Unintended Consequences
Anticipate second-order effects.
- What happens next?
- Who else is affected?
- What behaviors will this incentivize?
- Time horizons: Immediate vs. delayed effects

### Boundaries and Scopes
Define what's inside/outside the system.
- What are we including/excluding?
- At what level are we intervening?
- Are we solving symptoms or root causes?

## Probabilistic Reasoning

### Expected Value
Weight outcomes by likelihood.
- EV = Σ(probability × outcome value)
- Consider full distribution, not just average
- Account for tail risks and asymmetries

### Bayes' Theorem
Update beliefs with new evidence.
- Prior probability × Likelihood ratio = Posterior probability
- Ask: "How much should this evidence change my belief?"

### Base Rates
Start with population-level frequencies.
- Avoid representativeness bias
- "What % of [population] has [property]?"
- Anchor on base rate before personalizing

### Antifragility
Prefer options that benefit from volatility.
- Upside > Downside (convex payoffs)
- Multiple small bets over one large bet
- Options that gain from disorder

## Constraint Analysis

### Theory of Constraints
Find and exploit bottlenecks.
1. Identify the constraint
2. Exploit it (maximize throughput)
3. Subordinate everything else
4. Elevate the constraint
5. Repeat (find next constraint)

### Resource Allocation
Where does limiting factor live?
- Time, money, attention, energy, expertise
- Can we remove/relax the constraint?
- Can we shift to less constrained approach?

### Pareto Analysis
80/20 principle - find vital few.
- Which 20% creates 80% of value/problems?
- Focus efforts on high-leverage areas
- Cut or automate low-value activities
